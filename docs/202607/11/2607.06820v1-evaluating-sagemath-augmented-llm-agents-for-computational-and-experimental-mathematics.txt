# Abstract 

Recent advances in AI for Mathematics have fo-cused largely on autoformalization and theorem proving, leaving the role of Computer Algebra Systems (CAS) in agentic LLM workflows un-derexplored. We propose a ReAct-style agentic setup that combines LLM reasoning with veri-fiable feedback from SageMath, together with Context7 for the up-to-date documentation. We evaluate this agentic setup across frontier models for solving research-level mathematical problems from the RealMath benchmark in a setting that emulates a computational-mathematics research loop. We also propose a refinement to the Real-Math benchmark by introducing a multi-step post-processing procedure and a multi-stage validation pipeline, both of which improve the quality and reliability of the extracted problem set. Our exper-iments reveal substantial performance gains from SageMath access across all evaluated models on +9.7 pp on average, the gains range from 1.5 pp to 27.8 pp and narrow the gap between open-weight and closed models. Qwen 3.7-Max benefits from SageMath the most, while GPT-5.5 achieves the highest solve rate of 75 .2% and the lowest token usage among tool-enabled configurations. Our findings suggest that CAS-augmented agents rep-resent a promising direction for assisting mathe-maticians in computational exploration, and we believe that this work is a step towards automated conjecture discovery. The project repository is available online. 1

# 1. Introduction 

Recent progress in LLMs and agentic systems that integrate LLM reasoning with deterministic, verifiable tool backends such as compilers, theorem provers, SAT/SMT solvers, type checkers, and physical simulators has established a new neuro-symbolic paradigm in which generative reasoning is partially grounded in verifiable feedback. By coupling LLM reasoning with components that provide ground-truth signals, this paradigm enables a class of tools capable of automating tasks that previously required substantial expert effort and of producing answers with a level of reliability that purely generative approaches cannot achieve. A particularly active application of this paradigm is in math-ematics. Substantial progress has been made on autofor-malization, the translation of mathematical statements into the formal programs written in proof-assistant languages such as Lean, Coq, and Isabelle (Wu et al., 2022), as well as on automated theorem proving, where LLM-based sys-tems guide the search for formal proofs (Lin et al., 2025), attain gold-medal-level performance on the 2025 Interna-tional Mathematical Olympiad with formally verified so-lutions (Achim et al., 2025), and and have recently au-tonomously resolved several open problems from the Erd ˝os collection (Sothanaphan, 2026; Tsoukalas et al., 2026) in-cluding the well-known unit-distance conjecture (OpenAI, 2026). These developments establish the integration of LLMs with formal proof assistants as a promising direc-tion for mathematical reasoning, focused on proving and formalizing already specified statements. In many areas of mathematics, Computer Algebra Systems (CAS) and symbolic engines are routinely used for hypothe-sis exploration, candidate validation, and counterexample search. A common workflow in modern mathematical re-search, particularly in computational areas such as combina-torial commutative algebra, algebraic combinatorics, graph theory, finite group theory, and related fields, relies heav-ily on CAS. Prominent examples include SageMath (The Sage Developers, 2022), Macaulay2 (Grayson & Stillman), GAP (GAP, 2026), Magma (Bosma et al., 1997), PAR-I/GP (The PARI Group, 2023) and Singular (Decker et al., 2024) which provide computational support for research-level mathematical problem solving. Researchers use such 1arXiv:2607.06820v1 [cs.AI] 7 Jul 2026 Evaluating SageMath-Augmented LLM Agents for Computational and Experimental Mathematics 

systems to discover and prune the conjecture space by itera-tively computing examples, identifying patterns, formulat-ing tentative conjectures, and testing them computationally 2.This workflow are common in computational and experi-mentally driven areas of mathematics. Despite the widespread use of CAS, their integration into LLM-based systems for mathematical reasoning has re-ceived substantially less attention than the integration of formal proof assistants. In particular, existing work has focused largely on formal proof construction, leaving com-putational workflows based on symbolic experimentation and executable verification comparatively underexplored. This gap, together with the recent successes of LLMs in mathematical reasoning, motivates the present study. The central question of our work is the following: to what extent does verifiable symbolic feedback improve the perfor-mance of frontier LLMs on research-level mathematics, and does it shape their behavior in agentic setup? To study this effect, we evaluate frontier LLMs within an agentic setup with an access to SageMath that closely emulates a mathe-matician’s computational workflow. The proposed setup is particularly relevant for tasks where symbolic computation plays a central role and where core mathematical structures have already been algorithmically formalized within exist-ing software systems. Our contributions are threefold: 

Tool-use agentic evaluation: we present an empirical study of frontier LLMs in a ReAct-style agentic setup with multi-turn access to SageMath together with up-to-date documen-tation retrieval through Context7. We evaluate how verifi-able feedback from a CAS affects model performance on research-level mathematical problems through a controlled tool-access ablation. 

Benchmark refinement and evaluation protocol: building on the RealMath benchmark, we curate an evaluation set restricted to numerical and symbolic-expression answers. We additionally enrich problem statements with compressed contextual information and introduce an LLM-assisted nor-malization pipeline for executable symbolic verification. We further employ a hybrid validation protocol combin-ing symbolic equivalence checking with LLM-as-a-Judge re-evaluation. 

Error and behavioral analysis: we complement the quan-titative evaluation with an analysis of execution failures, recovery dynamics, and tool-call traces, highlighting model-specific patterns in how agents interact with symbolic com-putation tools. We also present a case study with a descrip-tion of the solution strategy used by agent to solve one of the problem.

# 3. Experimental setup 

We evaluate each model under two matched conditions: a tool-free baseline, in which the model solves each problem using direct reasoning alone, and a tool-augmented agentic setting, in which it can iteratively query SageMath and Context7. Apart from tool access and the corresponding system prompt, the base model and problem set are held fixed. This design isolates the effect of verifiable symbolic feedback on performance in research-level mathematics. We evaluate on a curated set of 133 problems from the RealMath benchmark and validate model outputs using a multi-stage protocol that combines symbolic equivalence checking with an LLM-as-a-judge stage. The system prompts for both pipelines are provided in Appendices F.1 and F.2. 

3.1. Evaluation Dataset 

We extracted a curated subset of 133 problems by per-forming additional processing steps, multi-stage filtering, and sampling from the RealMath benchmark (Zhang et al., 2025), a dataset of research-level mathematical problems that is directly derived from the contemporary literature. The additional steps are crucial, since we focus on exe-cutable mathematical reasoning and thus require problems to have algorithmically verifiable solutions. However, Real-Math was developed as a Q&A benchmark, in which the 

answer field is represented as plain LaTeX text, which prevents direct automatic comparison. Moreover, it was collected without assessing the computational perspective of the problems, thus it might contain problems that are not directly suitable for symbolic computation within the SageMath framework. We therefore construct the final evaluation suite through the following multi-stage pipeline (see Appendix A.2 for the full details regarding the dataset construction, filtering criteria, and annotation prompts): 1. We retain only those problems that can be normalized into SymPy objects that are suitable for automatic veri-fication. 2. We use LLM-assisted filtering to identify problems for which the symbolic experimentation or computational verification is feasible within SageMath. 3. We sample from the filtered pool and extend it with the problems from underrepresented arXiv categories. We also augment problem statements with the definitions and auxiliary statements that are crucial to interpret the question. 3Evaluating SageMath-Augmented LLM Agents for Computational and Experimental Mathematics

3.2. Agentic Setup 

The agentic pipeline is organized to emulate the workflow of a working mathematician. In the tool-free setting, the model relies on its internal knowledge resources and reasoning capabilities. However, the reasoning aimed to tackle a com-plicated mathematical problem might require intermediate computational steps with verifiable feedback. It would allow the agent to refine its reasoning through iterative experimen-tation and explore the hypothesis space more efficiently. We implement this pipeline by wrapping the models in a ReAct-style multi-turn loop, in which the agent repeatedly reasons about the current state, invokes an external tool, and incorporates the returned observation into the next turn. The tool interface provides access to SageMath for symbolic and computational mathematics and to Context7 for retrieving relevant library documentation. 

SageMath. SageMath (Sage) is a widely used open-source computer algebra system that provides a unified Python-based interface to a broad collection of mathematical software systems, including GAP, Singular, and PARI/GP. It supports symbolic computation, algebraic manipulation, and numerical experimentation, and provides extensive function-ality for polynomial ideals, graph-theoretic computations, number theory, and related areas. In our pipeline, the agent can generate Sage code snippets, execute them in an isolated sandbox (see Appendix A.1), observe the returned output, and iterate on its hypotheses. 

Context7. Context7 (Upstash Team, 2026) is a project that provides access to up-to-date documentation and code examples from official sources. The agent is given access to this tool to reduce hallucinations about modules, paths, and outdated API signatures, as the models may rely on outdated information about SageMath APIs acquired during pretraining. Providing an access to that tool, we mitigate the possibility that models might not know how to write Sage code, focusing on the model’s capability for executable, ver-ifiable thinking and its ability to refine candidate solutions. 

3.3. Experimental protocol 

We evaluate the agentic setup by comparing it with a tool-free baseline to measure the impact of tool integration on performance. To ensure a controlled ablation, we keep the underlying model and problem instances fixed across con-ditions, only tool access and the associated system prompt differ. In the agentic setting, we impose a hard cap of 15 tool calls per problem. We also require the model to return SymPy-parsable output, since the evaluation is based on symbolic verification. 

Tool-free setting. The tool-free pipeline consists of direct reasoning performed by a model without external feedback. It allows us to ablate the model’s internal capabilities for solving research-level mathematical problems. This setting is similar to standard direct-answer evaluation used in the original RealMath benchmark. 4Evaluating SageMath-Augmented LLM Agents for Computational and Experimental Mathematics

3.4. Evaluation pipeline 

Validating the equivalence of symbolic mathematical ex-pressions is substantially more challenging than validating numerical answers, since structurally different expressions may still be mathematically equivalent. Prior benchmarks address this issue either through symbolic equivalence pro-cedures (Shalyt et al., 2025) or through LLM-based judg-ing (Tsoukalas et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025). We combine both approaches in a multi-stage evaluation pipeline, as sym-bolic simplification is not exhaustive and may incorrectly reject mathematically equivalent expressions. 

Symbolic validator. The first stage compares the SymPy-parsable output produced by the model against the ground-truth answer represented in the same format. Expressions are parsed into SymPy objects and their difference is sim-plified symbolically. If the resulting expression simplifies to zero, the prediction is considered correct. For multi-component answers, outputs are compared component-wise. 

LLM-as-a-Judge validator. Cases rejected by the sym-bolic validator are not necessarily incorrect. For such cases, we employ a second-stage evaluation procedure based on three frontier LLM judges: Claude Opus 4.7, GPT-5.5, and Gemini 3.5 Flash 3. Each model independently produces a verdict together with a short justification, and the final decision is obtained through majority voting. In addition, we manually inspect 30% of the cases reaching this stage and treat the resulting human annotations as ground truth. 

Evaluation metric. We report solve rate: the fraction of problems whose final answer is accepted by the validation pipeline. Predictions are accepted either by symbolic equiv-alence checking or, when this is inconclusive, by majority vote among three LLM judges. Solve rate estimates are accompanied by 95% Wilson score confidence intervals.

Validation stage analysis. The hybrid validation pipeline behaved consistently across both evaluation settings. The symbolic checker directly accepted 39 .2% of all 3,990 pre-dictions; the remaining 60 .8% were routed to the LLM-judge panel, which overturned the rejection in 30 .8% of these cases ( 747 answers) and confirmed it in the rest. These recoveries overwhelmingly correspond to mathematically equivalent answers expressed in different canonical forms, so the judge acts primarily as a recovery mechanism for symbolic-verification failures rather than as an independent source of supervision. The panel itself is highly consis-tent: 97 .2% of its verdicts are unanimous across the three judges. Taking the final pipeline verdict as reference, the symbolic checker alone already produces the correct deci-sion in 81 .3% of cases. A more robust symbolic checker, one that handles a wider range of equivalent forms, could therefore close most of the remaining gap and enable fully deterministic validation of numerical and expression-typed answers, removing the need for LLM judges.

4.3. Limitations 

The present study has several limitations. First, the symbolic-verification procedure may fail to recognize cor-rect answers, since robust universal equivalence checking remains difficult in the general case; the judge stage recov-ers 23 –44% of credited solutions, so small inter-model gaps are methodologically fragile. Second, both dataset post-processing and secondary validation rely on LLM outputs, which are not fully reliable. Third, the experimental dataset is limited in size and restricted to numerical or symbolic-expression answers that can be normalized and automati-cally checked, introducing selection bias toward the compu-tationally executable subset of research-level mathematics. Finally, the tool-augmented setting combines SageMath, Context7, ReAct prompting, and a tool-use budget; thus, gains cannot be attributed to CAS access alone.

# 6. Conclusions 

We evaluated 15 frontier LLMs on 133 research-level math-ematical problems derived from the RealMath benchmark, comparing direct-answer inference against a ReAct-style agentic pipeline equipped with SageMath and Context7 doc-umentation retrieval. Tool access improved the solve rate of every evaluated model, by +9 .7 pp on average, with gains ranging from +1 .5 pp (Kimi K2.7) to +27 .8 pp (Qwen 3.7-Max, improving from 42 .1% to 69 .9% ). OpenAI’s flagship model, GPT-5.5, achieved the highest solve rate of 75 .2% at the lowest token usage among tool-enabled configurations, dominating every other model on both axes; it is followed by Opus 4.8 ( 73 .7% ) and the multi-agent orchestration system Fugu-Ultra, which matches Opus 4.7 at 72 .9% while being the second most token-efficient configuration. The CAS access substantially narrowed the gap between open-weight and closed frontier models. 12 Evaluating SageMath-Augmented LLM Agents for Computational and Experimental Mathematics 

Beyond overall solve rates, our experiments reveal consis-tent behavioral patterns that separate effective and ineffec-tive tool-augmented agents. Gains are non-uniform: open-weight models improve more than twice as much as closed models on average, largely due to weaker tool-free baselines, yet higher token expenditure does not translate into higher accuracy, and the token-efficiency ranking inverts between setups: open-weight models consume the fewest tokens at direct answering but the most as agents. Agentic runs are strongly bimodal: strong models solve a problem within the first 3–4 tool interactions, using SageMath mainly to verify solutions already derived through reasoning, whereas weaker agents exhaust the entire tool budget searching for a solution through computation, writing long-running code. Consistently, recovery dynamics after failed tool calls vary far more across models than raw failure rates, constituting a axis separating effective and ineffective agents. More broadly, our results support the view that computer algebra systems provide a promising substrate for computa-tional and experimental mathematics with LLM agents. Our case study shows that a CAS-augmented agent can repro-duce key elements of a working mathematician’s workflow: computing accessible intermediate objects, extracting pat-terns from examples, formulating conjectures, and validat-ing them across parameters. While current systems remain limited on problems requiring deep theoretical insight or specialized contemporary knowledge, the observed gains indicate that executable symbolic computation can substan-tially extend the practical reasoning capabilities of frontier models, and we view this direction as a step toward au-tomated mathematical experimentation and computational conjecture discovery. As future work, we propose a multi-agent architecture that assigns distinct responsibilities to agents equipped with a diverse set of tools. 

# Impact Statement 

This work studies CAS-assisted LLM agents for research mathematics. It advances verifiable AI-assisted scientific reasoning; we foresee no specific societal risks beyond those already recognized and release our evaluation set and vali-dation pipeline for reproducibility.

# A. Experimental Details 

A.1. Implementation Details SageMath Sandbox. All SageMath execution is performed in an ephemeral Docker container. For each tool call, a generated Sage program is launched through a small wrapper. This wrapper imports sage.all , runs Sage’s preparser to standardize Sage syntax, executes the script and extracts the designated result variable. It also captures standard output and returns a JSON-friendly representation of the result to the host. For each tool call, the generated code snippet is executed in a fresh interpreter. The execution is limited to 2 CPU cores, 4 GB of memory, a 45s wall-clock timeout, and a 50s CPU-time limit. The executed code has no network access. The host also rejects outputs larger than 262,144 bytes before parsing the returned result to prevent excessively large intermediate outputs. Failure handling is normalized into a small number of categories. User-code exceptions are returned as code error 

together with the captured traceback. Docker startup failures, such as an unavailable daemon or missing image, are classified as docker error . Container crashes or abnormal exits are classified as runtime crash , and wall-clock overruns are classified as timeout ; in the timeout case the host forcibly removes the container. 

A.2. Dataset Construction 

We begin with the dataset of 633 problems obtained from the RealMath benchmark (Zhang et al., 2025). We focus on this dataset because it is derived from the contemporary mathematical literature. Each problem from this dataset contains the question, context and the answer, each of which is represented in a LaTeX format. We also enrich the dataset enriching it with the arXiv categories that were associated with the papers from which the corresponding problems were scraped via the RealMath extraction pipeline. If the paper had several categories, only the first one was used. We automatically retain only those tasks whose final answers can be normalized into executable SymPy (Meurer et al., 2017) objects suitable for automatic verification. Specifically, we want the answers to be normalized into either a concrete numerical value, or a symbolic expression that might consist of several free variables or use the widely used mathematical notation (such as π for 3.14159 ... , e for 2.71828 ... , Γ for the Gamma function, etc). In order to do this, we employ LLM-assisted normalization procedure, under which the original ground truth answer, given in the free LaTeX form, is transformed into SymPy-parsable string. Under this procedure we reduce the problem size in half, resulting in 300 problems. Since the context field in RealMath often contains excessive or irrelevant information, we use an LLM to compress the context, retaining only the relevant definitions and incorporating them directly into the question. We automatically annotate the answer type as one of the following: a number is a concrete numerical value, an expression is a symbolic expression in some free variables, a formula is an equality “named invariant = expression” in which the left-hand side is a label rather than a checkable quantity. As a next step, we employ an LLM-assisted annotation stage to estimate whether symbolic experimentation or computational verification is feasible within SageMath. In order to do this, we define several criteria for a problem to be suitable for our study: a task is suitable if 1. Objects mentioned in the question can be represented by the means of SageMath, 2. Candidate answers can be explored with the help of computational experiment, 3. Intermediate computation steps can be executed in SageMath, This stage reduces the problem set to 219. From this, we sample 133 problems attempting to reduce the skewness of the distribution across arXiv categories.
