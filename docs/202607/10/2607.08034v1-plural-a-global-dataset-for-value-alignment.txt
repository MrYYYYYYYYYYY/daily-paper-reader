# PLURAL: A Global Dataset for Value Alignment 

Dhruv Agarwal ∗, Anya Shukla, Tanya Goyal & Aditya Vashistha 

Cornell University 

## Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) are used worldwide, yet disproportion-ately reflect Western values, limiting their ability to represent diverse value systems. We introduce PLURAL , a large-scale, value-focused preference dataset grounded in the Integrated Values Survey (IVS), a nationally rep-resentative survey spanning 92 countries. Using a two-stage generation pipeline, we transform survey responses into synthetic preference triplets that preserve normative value signals while producing realistic scenarios. We release an initial version of PLURAL containing ∼500,000 preference triplets representing people in 20 diverse countries. We evaluate PLURAL in three ways: (i) dataset-level validation showing that it preserves both cross-country value differences and within-country diversity from the orig-inal survey; (ii) automated evaluation showing that training on PLURAL improves alignment with target countries’ cultural profiles, reducing mean absolute error by up to 27.7% relative to strong baselines; and (iii) blind human evaluation with 176 evaluators in India, Brazil, and Japan, who judge PLURAL-aligned responses as more representative of their national values. Together, these results show that PLURAL contains learnable signal for value steering, offering a scalable resource for pluralistic alignment. 

Dataset: huggingface.co/datasets/agdhruv/plural-alignment 

## 1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) increasingly mediate everyday tasks from writing assistance to complex decision-making, raising concerns about whose values they reflect (Prabhakaran et al., 2022). A growing body of work shows that these models disproportionately encode Western cultures and values (Johnson et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023; Qadri et al., 2023). This Western-centric anchoring can produce tangible harms for international users, such as homogenizing values during writing (Agarwal et al., 2025a; Shahid et al., 2026). To address these representational harms, the AI community has increasingly focused on developing regional or “sovereign” language models tailored to local languages and commu-nities, such as Nanda for Hindi (Choudhury et al., 2025) and Jais for Arabic (Sengupta et al., 2023). Yet, recent work shows that regional models are often “fluent but foreign”: they learn local syntax yet continue to reflect American cultural values, highlighting that achieving linguistic fluency in a target language does not guarantee value alignment (Agarwal et al., 2025b). Thus, the challenge is not merely to build models that speak different languages, but to build models that can engage with the plurality of human values across cultures, a broader problem formalized as pluralistic alignment (Sorensen et al., 2024). Existing alignment efforts typically rely on preference-learning methods such as RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022) and DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024), which depend on human preference data to steer model behavior. In practice, however, such data have historically been collected disproportionately from Western, English-speaking populations, thus op-timizing models for relatively narrow demographic slices (Bai et al., 2022). More recent efforts such as PRISM and Community Alignment broaden representation, but important limitations remain. (2) We introduce a pipeline for transforming survey responses into high-quality synthetic preference data while preserving cross- and within-country value signal. (3) We show, through experiments on five countries, that PLURAL can steer LLMs toward more culturally representative behavior. 

## 2 The PLURAL Dataset 

Our goal is to build a value-focused dataset that can capture diverse cultural values and serve as a scalable resource for pluralistic alignment. Because obtaining global scale through crowdsourcing is challenging, we instead ground our dataset in validated social science methodologies, ensuring representative populations within each country. 

2.1 Grounding in Large-Scale Value Surveys 

We construct PLURAL using the Integrated Values Survey (IVS) (EVS/WVS, 2024), a large dataset containing responses to value-related questions from 156,658 respondents across 92 2Preprint. Under review. countries and territories. IVS is a credible resource for value alignment data because it is the product of decades of methodological refinement in the social sciences. IVS combines data from two historically distinct programs: the World Values Survey (WVS) and the European Values Study (EVS). In the 2017–2022 round (WVS Wave 7, EVS Wave 5), the two coordinated data collection with a shared core questionnaire, with EVS covering Europe and WVS covering the rest of the world (Haerpfer et al., 2024; EVS, 2022). This broad geographic coverage is one of the key reasons we use IVS; while the joint dataset contains fewer questions than the standalone datasets, it covers a wider range of countries, giving a broader view of human values. Both EVS and WVS are long-running programs and follow rigorous cross-national survey practices, including standardized instruments, trained interviewers, face-to-face data collec-tion, and translation into widely spoken languages. They rely on nationally representative samples (typically n ≥ 1, 200) and robust internal consistency checks. We therefore use IVS as the seed for synthetic data generation, as its scale and rigor provide a credible foundation for global value alignment beyond what any feasible annotation effort could achieve. 

2.2 Pre-Processing Survey Data Preserving Survey Structure. In the IVS release, each survey item appears as a separate column, even when several items were originally asked together under the same survey prompt. For example, questions about desirable qualities for children (e.g., independence, obedience) are recorded separately in the data, but are presented together in the survey as part of a single item battery. To preserve this original survey structure, we manually reconstruct groups of related questions using the survey documentation and use them as the basic unit of curation in the rest of our pipeline. This yields 59 question groups. 

Isolating Normative Values. Not all 59 groups capture normative values. Following Rokeach’s hierarchy of beliefs (Rokeach, 1973), we label each group as prescriptive (what people ought to do, e.g., prioritizing family vs. leisure), descriptive (perceptions of the world, e.g., whether elections are fair), or primitive (personal practices, e.g., frequency of religious attendance). We retain only prescriptive beliefs as they are most relevant to AI behavior (Huang et al., 2025); we exclude descriptive and primitive beliefs because they do not specify what AI should recommend in a value-laden dilemma. This removes 18 groups, leaving 41 for data generation. Details about Rokeach’s framework, filtering methodology, and validation are provided in Appendix A.

Stage 1: Triplet Generation. For each participant and question group, we provide the LLM with (1) the participant’s demographic profile, (2) the relevant survey questions, and (3) their responses. The LLM converts this structured input into a realistic scenario that preserves the normative tension implied by the participant’s answers, with demographics grounding the scenario in a personal context. Thus, it generates a triplet comprising: (i) a user prompt describing the scenario, (ii) a preferred response aligned with the participant’s answers, and (iii) a dispreferred response reflecting a plausible but contrasting stance. Following prior work (Troshin et al., 2025), we generate five triplets per question group in a single call to promote scenario diversity while keeping the underlying value orientation fixed. 

Stage 2: Response Expansion. Stage 1 tends to produce terse responses atypical of AI assistants in conversational settings, likely due to the constrained generation prompt. We therefore pass each preferred and dispreferred response through a second prompt that expands it into a more natural, assistant-like answer while explicitly maintaining the under-lying value stance. This yields more natural, conversational data for training. 

Ensuring Quality. First, triplet generation is performed over question groups rather than individual items, so the model can condition on multiple related answers when inferring a participant’s values. For example, jointly considering how important a participant finds family, work, and leisure gives a clearer picture of their priorities than treating each response independently. Second, to reduce model-specific bias, we randomly choose from a pool of frontier LLMs (GPT-5 Mini, Mistral Large 3, DeepSeek-v3.2) and use stochastic decoding to increase stylistic diversity. Third, all triplets are generated in English; models can be linguistically fluent yet culturally misaligned (Agarwal et al., 2025b), so standardizing the language reduces surface-level linguistic variation, isolating values. Finally, since the dataset is generated synthetically, we manually verify that it is faithful to IVS responses and contains rich cultural artifacts rather than generic stereotypes (Appendix F.1). 

## 3 Evaluation Methodology 

In this work, we do not argue for a specific operationalization of value-pluralistic LLMs (e.g., one globally conditioned model versus country-specific adapters). Instead, our goal is to ask: 

does our dataset contain enough signal to train models to reflect population-level value orientations? 

We answer this question using three complementary evaluations: a dataset-level validation, an automated evaluation of PLURAL-aligned models, and a human evaluation of down-stream model behavior. We focus our evaluation on five countries selected as a diverse evaluation set spanning multiple regions: India, Brazil, Japan, Malaysia, and Zimbabwe. 

3.1 Dataset-Level Validation 

Before evaluating downstream aligned models, we first ask whether PLURAL preserves the value structure present in the original IVS data. We assess this in two ways using respondent-level value representations derived from google/embeddinggemma-300m (Vera et al., 2025). First, we test whether value differences between countries are retained by asking whether a respondent’s country can be predicted from their value representation. Second, we test 4Preprint. Under review. whether PLURAL preserves within-country diversity by comparing normalized distributions of pairwise respondent distances within each country before and after synthetic generation. Full details are in Appendix E.1. 

3.2 DPO Fine-Tuning Setup 

We next evaluate whether the value preferences encoded in PLURAL can be learned by a language model. We test this by fine-tuning instruction-tuned base models on our synthetic preference triplets using Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024). For each target country, we train on triplets derived from respondents sampled from that country alone, yielding country-specific adapted models. We conduct our main experiments using llama-3.1-8b-instruct as the base model. We report additional results on other base models in Appendix F.4 to assess generalization across model families. DPO training details including hyperparameters are provided in Appendix E.2. 

3.3 Automated Evaluation with GLOBE 

To evaluate the DPO-tuned model, we use a cultural framework that is entirely distinct from the one used for training. The GLOBE framework (House et al., 2004) is a well-established framework that identifies nine cultural dimensions along which societies differ (e.g., Uncertainty Avoidance, Future Orientation, and Performance Orientation) based on data from over 17,300 middle managers across 62 societies. Because GLOBE is derived from a different population and conceptual framework than our training data, it allows us to assess whether learned value signals generalize beyond the original survey distribution. 

Benchmark. To adapt GLOBE for LLMs, we use the LLM-GLOBE benchmark (Karinshak et al., 2024), which probes models with 900 open-ended, real-world dilemmas (100 each for the nine dimensions). These scenarios elicit behavioral responses that reveal a model’s position along each dimension. For example, a question evaluating Performance Orientation might ask the model to choose between merit-based or equal bonuses for employees. This setup captures values through open-ended behavior rather than direct queries, which can diverge from actual actions (Shen et al., 2025). Responses are scored on a 1-7 scale using detailed rubrics for each dimension. Importantly, scores reflect the degree of alignment with a cultural dimension (e.g., high or low Performance Orientation), not correctness. 

Procedure. For each of the nine GLOBE dimensions, we use the corresponding 100 bench-mark prompts to generate open-ended responses from our model. Each response is then evaluated by an LLM judge, which assigns a score on a 1-7 scale using a dimension-specific rubric. We use GPT-5 Mini (medium reasoning) as the judge. Details on judge validation, as well as the full prompting and scoring procedure, are provided in Appendix E.3. 

Scoring and Metric. For each dimension, we compute the model’s mean score across its 100 responses. We then compare this mean to the target country’s ground-truth GLOBE score for that dimension using absolute error (AE). Our overall metric is mean absolute error (MAE), computed by averaging AE across all nine dimensions. Lower values indicate that the model’s responses more closely match the target country’s ground-truth cultural profile. A formal definition of the metric is provided in Appendix E.3.

We complement the automated downstream evaluation with a human evaluation in three countries: India, Brazil, and Japan. Since Prolific lacked sufficient participants in Malaysia and Zimbabwe, we could not run human evaluation for these countries.

They were recruited through Prolific, a crowd-sourcing platform, and compensated at $8 per hour. Procedure. We structure the human evaluation as a comparison between two models (e.g., a baseline model and our fine-tuned model). Using prompts from the LLM-GLOBE benchmark, we first generate responses from both models and select a subset of 36 prompts for which the automated score gap between them is largest. Because GLOBE scores reflect position along a cultural dimension rather than correctness, this selection increases the visibility of value differences without favoring either model. Each evaluator is shown 10 randomly selected prompts from this pool, along with two candidate responses generated by the models. The order of the responses is randomized, and both models are instructed to keep responses under 100 words to minimize presentation biases. For each prompt, evaluators answer the following question: “Which response is more typical of values commonly held in [Country]?” See Appendix E.4 for an example. Since obtaining nationally representative annotator pools is difficult at small scale, we ask evaluators to judge country-level typicality rather than personal agreement. This framing follows social science research that conceptualizes cultural alignment in terms of population-level tendencies rather than any individual’s values, which may naturally deviate from the cultural center (Akaliyski et al., 2021). 

3.5 Baselines 

We compare our model DPO-trained on PLURAL against four baselines that provide different kinds and amounts of cultural information to the model. This includes both prompting and fine-tuning baselines. Full prompts and pre-processing details are in App E.5. 

Vanilla model. The original, unsteered instruction-tuned model. We use a neutral system prompt (“You are a helpful assistant. Provide a decision along with a short explanation.”). This serves as the default reference point for all other comparisons. 

Demographic prompting (DP). We condition the model on the target country through prompting (e.g., “The user is from [Country]”). This tests whether lightweight prompting can elicit culturally aligned behavior without any training (Adilazuarda et al., 2024). 

Aggregate prompting (AP). We provide the model country-level cultural statistics—the target country’s scores on the nine GLOBE dimensions—in the system prompt. This is a strong baseline as it injects information from the evaluation criteria at inference time. 

Community Alignment DPO (CA). We train on the Community Alignment dataset (Zhang et al., 2026), a large-scale multilingual preference dataset covering 3,500 annotators across five countries. It is explicitly designed to capture variation in preferences across communi-ties, making it a natural baseline for our setting. These baselines evaluate the value signal captured by PLURAL. We also include a method-ological baseline, PersonaHub , to isolate the contribution of IVS grounding. ## 4 Results 

4.1 Dataset-Level Validation Preserves IVS cross-country value differences. As a reference, we first check value differ-ences between countries in the underlying IVS data: for each respondent (500 total; 100 per country), we construct a one-hot feature vector of all their IVS responses and train a logistic classifier to predict country. This classifier achieves 89.4% accuracy ( ±2.3; chance = 20%), indicating that respondents from different countries exhibit distinct values. We then test whether these differences persist after converting survey responses into prefer-ence data. We embed each respondent’s preferred and dispreferred responses from PLURAL (up to 5000 per country) and average their difference to obtain a respondent-level value representation. Training the same classifier on these representations achieves 78.0% accuracy (±4.2), well above chance, indicating that PLURAL preserves substantial country-specific value differences even after synthetic generation. We additionally verify using PCA that these results are robust under dimensionality reduction, indicating that performance is not driven by high-dimensionality overfitting. 

Preserves within-country value diversity. For each country, we compare the distribution of pairwise cosine distances between respondents in IVS and in PLURAL. Figure 3 shows that these distributions align closely across all five countries, with an average Wasserstein distance of 0.051, indicating a mismatch of only 5% of the average pairwise distance. Second, we check for “template collapse”: does synthetic generation collapse into a small set of repeated questions (e.g., always mapping “elderly Japanese male who values family” to the same scenario)? The analysis in Appendix F.2 confirms no significant template collapse. 

4.2 Automated Evaluation Improves value alignment across countries. Our model outperforms the vanilla baseline across all five countries. As shown in Figure 4, fine-tuning on PLURAL reduces MAE to the target country’s GLOBE profile by 15.7% (Japan) to 27.7% (India), with statistically significant gains in all cases. This indicates that PLURAL captures learnable country-level value signal that generalizes to an external framework. These gains do not come at the expense of general capabilities, as measured by MMLU (App. F.3). By contrast, demographic prompting (DP) yields small, non-significant gains (1.4%-6.3%), while aggregate prompting (AP) is inconsistent and often fails to outperform the vanilla model despite having access to country-level information. This suggests that prompting alone is less effective than learning from grounded preference data. CA shows moderate gains in India but not in Brazil (despite more training rows; App. E.5), and is unavailable for other countries as it covers only five countries (vs. 20 in PLURAL). 

Improvements across most cultural dimensions. To better understand the gains in Figure 4, we break down performance by GLOBE dimension in Figure 5. The bar plot shows that, averaged across the five countries, our model improves on 7 of the 9 GLOBE dimensions: Performance Orientation (PO), Power Distance (PD), Assertiveness (ASS), In-group Col-lectivism (IGC), Future Orientation (FO), and Uncertainty Avoidance (UA). Institutional Collectivism (IC) improves modestly but less consistently. By contrast, Humane Orientation (HO; worsens) and Gender Egalitarianism (GE; minimal change) are more resistant. question ) + ( 1 | country ), where chosen ours indicates whether our model was preferred over the baseline. These failures manifest in various downstream use cases, including writing assistance (Agarwal et al., 2025a), text-to-image generation (Qadri et al., 2023), and cross-cultural understanding (Guo et al., 2025b). Researchers have developed a range of evaluation methods and benchmarks to measure such biases in LLMs. These benchmarks evaluate LLMs’ cultural value systems (Karinshak et al., 2024; Chiu et al., 2025), cultural adaptability (Rao et al., 2025), values across socially sensitive topics (Pistilli et al., 2024), values associated with specific regions (Zahraei & Asgari, 2025; Naous et al., 2024), and the intersection between personality traits and cultural values (Dey et al., 2025). However, while this line of work has been instrumental in establishing and characterizing cultural misalignment in LLMs, it does not directly address how to mitigate or resolve this issue. 

Preference Datasets. Preference datasets are a well-known tool for LLM alignment (Ouyang et al., 2022). Preference datasets range in focus, from safety and harmlessness (Bai et al., 2022; Ji et al., 2025), to cultural alignment (Guo et al., 2025a; Tasawong et al., 2026), and cultural pluralism (Zhang et al., 2026; Kirk et al., 2024). However, existing datasets are limited in coverage, thus restricting their use. The Community Alignment dataset (Zhang et al., 2026) contains comparisons from only five countries, and PRISM (Kirk et al., 2024) is heavily skewed towards white-identifying annotators from the United States and United Kingdom. In contrast, PLURAL contains nationally-representative data from 20 countries. 

Pluralistic AI. As AI systems are increasingly deployed worldwide, they must grapple with pluralism : the need to engage with diverse, and sometimes conflicting, human values. Sorensen et al. (2024) articulate this agenda, distinguishing among systems that expose a range of value orientations, can be steered toward different values, or reflect the value distribution of a population. Prior work has approached this challenge through methods such as multi-agent collaboration (Feng et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2026), self-generated plu-ralistic fine-tuning data (Xu et al., 2024), and retrieval-based conditioning on cultural and demographic information (Seo et al., 2025). Some approaches use WVS as a seed to generate culturally-aware training data, but rely only on questions , prompting models to generate responses from their latent cultural knowledge (Li et al., 2024b)—which can be incomplete or stereotyped. Others aggregate country-level signals, failing to preserve within-country variation (Li et al., 2024a). In contrast, PLURAL is grounded directly in human survey responses, enabling future alignment methods to capture population-level value diversity. 

## 6 Discussion 

PLURAL contains signal, but post-training compresses diversity. Our results show that country-specific DPO on PLURAL improves alignment to target countries across both automated and human evaluations. However, further analysis in Appendix F.6 shows that adapted models remain substantially more similar to one another than the corresponding country profiles. In particular, while ground-truth country profiles are widely separated in GLOBE space, the adapted models cluster tightly and preserve only ∼18% of that variation. Using supervised fine-tuning (SFT) instead of DPO increases retained variation to 30%, suggesting that compression is not an upper bound imposed by PLURAL. Together with the dataset-level validation in Section 4.1, this indicates that PLURAL contains cross-country signal, but current post-training methods only partially preserve it. This is consistent with prior work showing reduced diversity in preference optimization (Li et al., 2025; Lanchantin et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2026). Thus, while PLURAL provides sufficient country-level signal, current post-training methods can erase each country’s distinctiveness. This highlights the need for approaches that better preserve plurality. 

What is the right operationalization of pluralistic alignment? Human values are hetero-geneous, contextual, and often contested even within the same society. Our experiments use population-level country averages as the alignment target, but this is only one opera-tionalization of pluralistic alignment. In practice, alignment may need to operate across multiple levels, raising questions about when a model should reflect broad country-level tendencies, adapt to individuals or sub-communities, or stay neutral (Sorensen et al., 2024). 9Preprint. Under review. PLURAL enables researchers to explore this space by providing both country-level signal and detailed participant-level value and demographic profiles. 

Representation versus stereotyping. Even if a model can learn country-level value tenden-cies, deciding when and how to express them is challenging. A model that defaults to the statistical center of a user’s country risks stereotyping, while one that ignores population-level tendencies risks flattening meaningful cultural variation (Agarwal et al., 2025a). The boundary between representation and stereotyping is thus ambiguous. Rather than pre-scribing a default behavior, PLURAL enables this question to be studied empirically. 

Tension between cultural alignment and other alignment goals. In our dimension-level analysis, we find that Gender Egalitarianism changes little on average. One plausible explanation is that this dimension interacts directly with safety norms already embedded in current LLMs. For example, better matching some countries on Gender Egalitarianism may require generating responses that are less egalitarian by contemporary safety standards (e.g., endorsing unequal rights to work for men and women). This raises a fundamental question: does such behavior constitute faithful cultural representation, or the reproduction of harmful bias? We highlight this as a central challenge for pluralistic alignment: culturally representative behavior may conflict with what is normatively desirable. 

Limitations. GLOBE scores come from middle managers rather than nationally represen-tative populations; although the original survey justifies this design choice, it remains a limitation that we inherit. Our human evaluators also skew young and male; asking for country-level typicality mitigates this sampling bias but does not eliminate it. Finally, although our qualitative analysis suggests strong IVS grounding, synthetic generation can still introduce occasional culturally irrelevant details. 

## Ethics Statement 

We do not impose our values on the dataset. This means that if an IVS respondent strongly indicated in the IVS that bribery is justifiable or that men should have more rights to jobs than women, our pipeline faithfully generates data justifying those positions. PLURAL preserves values exactly as they appear in the IVS ground truth, faithfully mirroring global value systems. We leave the complex decisions regarding how to navigate conflicting or controversial values to the alignment community. The human evaluation was IRB-approved.

## A Filtering IVS Question Groups for Normative Beliefs 

As explained in Section 2, we filtered the 59 IVS question groups to retain only those that capture normative beliefs. To operationalize this distinction, we drew on Milton Rokeach’s hierarchy of beliefs. 

Rokeach’s Hierarchy of Beliefs. Rokeach’s hierarchy of beliefs is a foundational framework in the psychology of human values and has influenced later works, including the widely used Schwartz Theory of Basic Human Values (Schwartz, 1992; Smallenbroek et al., 2025), which builds directly on Rokeach and preserves the core idea that values are guiding principles for human behavior. We use Rokeach as a well-established tool for separating normative value judgments from other kinds of survey responses. In particular, Rokeach distinguishes among three broad categories. Primitive beliefs are foundational, often faith-based truths about the self or the world that are held with high certainty regardless of external evidence. Descriptive beliefs are perceptions or assessments about external reality, such as whether institutions are functioning fairly or whether society is changing in a particular direction. Prescriptive beliefs , which we treat as values, are enduring standards regarding how people ought to behave or what goals are worth pursuing. This distinction is especially relevant for our setting because our goal is to isolate normative value orientations that can guide model behavior, rather than factual perceptions or personal identities. 

Procedure. Each question group was independently labeled by two authors based on the underlying IVS questions and response options as primarily targeting prescriptive, descriptive, or primitive beliefs. The following definitions were used: Prescriptive Beliefs: Enduring standards or ideals regarding how people ought to behave or what ultimate goals are worth pursuing (e.g., children should be obedient). Descriptive Beliefs: Cognitive assessments or perceptions of external reality based on experience or information from the environment (e.g., whether an election is rigged). Primitive Beliefs: Foundational, faith-based truths about the nature of the self and the universe that are held with absolute certainty regardless of external evidence (e.g., belief in God). 

Validation. Across the 59 question groups, the two coders achieved 79.7% raw agreement and a Cohen’s κ of 0.65, indicating substantial agreement. We first retained all question groups that either coder labeled as “prescriptive”, yielding 35 groups. We then reviewed the remaining groups against the inclusion criterion: whether they signal a preference about how people ought to behave or what goals are worth pursuing, rather than simply factual perceptions or personal identities. Through this adjudication process, we retained 6 15 Preprint. Under review. additional groups whose responses and removed the remaining 18. This yielded 41 question groups for data generation. ## B Selecting Stratification Variables and Sample Size 

Applying our synthetic preference-generation pipeline to the full IVS would be computa-tionally expensive, since each respondent must be processed through multiple LLM calls. We therefore sample a smaller subset of respondents from each country, while aiming to preserve representativeness with respect to key demographic dimensions. 

Choosing stratification variables. We selected stratification dimensions using a data-driven criterion: they should explain meaningful variation in value responses while re-maining feasible for proportionate stratified sampling. For each candidate demographic variable and value variable combination, we computed the weighted between-group vari-ance explained ( R2) across value items. We then compared this explanatory signal against practical complexity, including the number of strata induced by the candidate partition and the extent of missing-data coverage. Based on this analysis, we chose the joint combination of sex , age , and education . Age was discretized into four bands (15–29, 30–44, 45–59, and 60+), and education was represented using the three-level IVS recoding. This combination explained substantially more value variation than lower-complexity alternatives such as sex+age, while avoiding the sparsity and reduced coverage of more fine-grained combinations. 

Estimating sample size. After fixing the stratification variables, we determined the sample size through Monte Carlo simulation. For each country, we constructed the weighted target distribution over the joint sex × age × education strata using the IVS survey weights. We then simulated repeated stratified proportionate samples at a range of candidate sample sizes ( N ∈ { 50, 60, 75, 100, 125, . . . , 500 }), and for each draw computed the Total Varia-tion Distance (TVD) between the sampled stratum distribution and the weighted target distribution. For each country and each candidate N, we ran 10,000 simulations and recorded the 99th percentile of the resulting TVD distribution. We selected the smallest N for which this quantity fell below 0.05. Across all 92 countries, the minimum acceptable sample size lay between 50 and 100. We therefore use a fixed sample size of 100 respondents per country in the final dataset. This choice is feasible because nearly all countries in the IVS contain at least 1,000 respondents, with the sole exception of Northern Ireland, which contains 447. Thus, in the final pipeline, respondents are sampled separately within each country using proportionate stratified sampling over the joint sex × age × education distribution. This gives a computationally manageable subset while maintaining close demographic alignment with the weighted national distribution.

## E Evaluation Details 

E.1 Dataset-Level Validation 

We provide additional details for the dataset-level validation described in Section 4.1. Our goal is to test whether PLURAL preserves both (i) value differences between countries, and (ii) diversity within each country, present in the original IVS data. 

Setup. We conduct all analyses on the same sampled set of 500 respondents (100 per country) spanning five countries: India, Brazil, Japan, Malaysia, and Zimbabwe. For IVS, each respondent is represented using one-hot encodings over the retained survey items (111 features across 41 question groups). For PLURAL, we sample 5000 rows per country and construct respondent-level representations by embedding preferred and dispreferred responses using google/embeddinggemma-300m , and averaging the difference between them across all samples for that respondent. This differencing is critical: (i) it cancels country-specific artifacts that may appear in the responses, and (ii) it isolates the respondent’s underlying value signal while removing prompt-specific variation. 

Cross-country value differences. To evaluate whether value differences between countries are preserved, we perform a country prediction task: can a classifier recover a respondent’s country using only their value representation. We train a multinomial logistic regression classifier (with 5-fold cross-validation for robustness) to predict country labels (5-way classification). High accuracy above random chance (20%) indicates that cross-country value differences are preserved, as otherwise respondents would not be distinguishable. 

Within-country diversity. We next test whether PLURAL preserves diversity within each country. For each country, we compute pairwise distances between all respondents us-ing respondent-level value representations derived from embeddings, and compare the resulting distance distributions between IVS and PLURAL. But since IVS and PLURAL representations live in different feature spaces, raw distances are not directly comparable. We therefore normalize distances within each dataset by dividing by the dataset-wide mean pairwise distance. This yields a scale-invariant measure of relative spread, allowing com-parisons across representations. Finally, for each country, we compare this distribution of normalized distance across IVS and PLURAL using Wasserstein distance. If the distributions align closely, it indicates that PLURAL preserves the within-country spread of values, rather than collapsing respondents toward a small number of stereotyped profiles. 18 Preprint. Under review. 

E.2 DPO Fine-Tuning Details 

We fine-tune base instruction-tuned models on PLURAL using HuggingFace TRL’s DPO implementation. Rather than updating the full model weights, we train LoRA adapters on top of the base model ( r = 16, α = 32, dropout = 0.05). We split the dataset into training and validation sets using a 95/5 split. Before training, we remove examples whose rendered prompts exceed 512 tokens. Prompts are rendered using the model’s chat template and filtered if the resulting tokenized prompt exceeds this limit. This is recommended by HuggingFace over truncating long prompts. We train on NVIDIA a single H100 GPU for one epoch with an effective batch size of 32 (per-device batch size of 8, gradient accumulation of 4 steps, and gradient checkpointing enabled). For our main experiments with llama-3.1-8b-instruct, we use a DPO β parameter of 0.1 and a learning rate of 5 × 10 −5.We emphasize that this training setup is intended as a simple testbed for whether PLURAL supports value steering. We do not claim that DPO with country-specific adapters represents the optimal or only way to operationalize value pluralism in future systems.

F.1 Qualitative Analysis of Generated Data The generated data is faithful to IVS rather than relying on stereotypes. Since the data generation relies on LLMs, we manually verify PLURAL is truly grounded in IVS participant data rather than reproducing generic stereotypes. We conducted a qualitative analysis of 50 randomly sampled triplets (10 per country). For each triplet, we assessed (a) value-groundedness: whether scenarios were grounded in IVS questions and participant responses, and (b) demographic-groundedness: whether they reflected IVS participant demographics (e.g., age, gender, occupation). We found that 98% were value-grounded (i.e., faithful to the IVS questions and aligned with the direction of the participants’ answers), and 78% were demographically-grounded (i.e., mentioned explicit demographic details). Manual annotations for the 50 random triplets are provided here. 

The data contains culturally-specific cues instead of generic artifacts. Additionally, the triplets contained culturally specific details like Malaysian history, Japanese cultural con-cepts ( oyakoko , meiwaku ), Indian digital services ( e-Nagrik ) and writers (Rabindranath Tagore), and Brazilian job-search portals and terminology such as bairro .
