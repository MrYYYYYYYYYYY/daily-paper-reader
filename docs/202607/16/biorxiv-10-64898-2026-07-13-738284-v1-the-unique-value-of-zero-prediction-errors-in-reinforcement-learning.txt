Abstract 

Learning is typically understood as a process driven by prediction errors, when outcomes differ from expectations. Yet it remains unclear whether outcomes that perfectly match expectations are psychologically and computationally meaningful. Here, we tested whether zero prediction errors shape affect, belief updating, and neural feedback processing in human reinforcement learning. Participants repeatedly predicted rewards in environments varying in uncertainty, with a subset of trial outcomes manipulated to exactly match their predictions. Zero prediction errors produced the highest momentary happiness, and computational modeling showed that behavior was best explained by a model in which zero prediction errors induce a distinct latent belief state that guides subsequent updating, particularly under higher uncertainty and in individuals with greater intolerance of uncertainty. Outcome-locked EEG analyses further showed that zero prediction errors elicited distinct P3-like responses, with residual neural activity predicting attenuated updating after zero prediction errors but enhanced updating after standard prediction errors. These findings suggest that perfect predictions are not neutral, but informative events that actively shape affect, behavior, and neural feedback processing. 

Introduction 

Through experience, we form beliefs that create expectations (“bus arrives at 9 am”). If the expectation is violated (bus arrived at 09:10 am), the belief is updated. If not, the belief is maintained. Accordingly, most learning is driven by a mismatch between expectations and reality. This basic principle was postulated by Rescorla & Wagner 1, and has become the basis of most learning models, from simple associations like classical conditioning 1–3 to more complex cognitive processes, such as social interactions 4–7 , financial choices 8,9 , cognitive control 10,11 , and working memory 12 . Sometimes, however, we get things surprisingly right. Perfect predictions can occur in stochastic outcomes (bus actually arrived at 9 am), where there is always a certain degree of uncertainty. We propose that human cognition is particularly attuned to these “zero prediction errors” so that when they occur, affective, behavioral, and neural processes react in a non-linear fashion that current models are not well equipped to capture. Taken together, this would suggest that perfect, reality-matching expectations constitute, ironically, a special case of error feedback processing. The impact of zero prediction errors on affect is relatively straightforward to anticipate if we consider that organisms are intrinsically motivated to reduce uncertainty. In humans, a canonical example is the certainty premium: subjective value increases non-linearly when an outcome changes from highly likely (probability = 0.90) to fully certain (probability = 1), suggesting that certainty itself carries value 13 . Similarly, large prediction errors and uncertain outcomes are experienced as aversive

predictability is also present in non-human animals; rats show heightened stress responses when aversive outcomes become unpredictable 16 . At a computational level, active inference accounts propose that organisms are fundamentally driven to minimize prediction error and uncertainty (Friston, 2010, 2013) and that a precise match between expectation and outcome signals successful resolution of uncertainty. All considered, zero prediction errors should be intrinsically rewarding, giving rise to positive affect 19,20 . Interestingly, in current models of momentary happiness 21,22 , zero prediction errors are treated as a regular instance of prediction errors, reflecting the common, non-special treatment zero prediction errors have received in the literature. Here, we contrast it to an alternative, happiness model that gives a non-linear weight to zero prediction errors. Our model can capture the simple effect that people feel the happiest when reality perfectly matches expectations. Zero prediction errors are likely to have a positive impact in our affective system: being right after all feels good. A more fundamental question is how people adapt to instances in which outcomes are perfectly predicted. As previously mentioned, classical models assume that learning occurs when reality does not match expectations, that is, when there are prediction errors 1,2,23 . Later, more sophisticated accounts show that prediction errors can impact internal representations beyond the general belief (“bus arrives at 9 am”) in, for example, the level of certainty around this belief 24–26 . In fact, zero prediction errors may be informative if we consider that belief updating depends also on the uncertainty of the prediction (“I think bus comes at 9 am, but I am not sure”). From this perspective, a zero prediction error may carry information about the latent model that generated the prediction, and, by that logic, drive behavioral change during learning. The main question then is which computational process better captures how people adapt to zero prediction errors. Inspired by recent work showing that large prediction errors trigger the formation of a separate latent belief state 27–29 , we postulate that a similar process follows suit for zero prediction errors. That is, zero prediction errors trigger the creation of a new latent state, which is solely responsible for tracking outcomes that led to zero prediction errors. Following this tenet, we introduce the Zero Prediction Error (ZePE) model, which assumes that people hold two latent states in parallel during learning: one for regular prediction errors, and another one for zero prediction errors, both of which inform the next prediction independently. For ZePE, zero prediction errors are thus a special instance of feedback processing, and, hence, non-linearly treated during learning. At the neural level, prediction error processing is relatively well characterized: midbrain dopaminergic neurons encode reward prediction errors, increasing firing for better-than-expected outcomes and decreasing firing for worse-than-expected outcomes 23,30–32 . Crucially, when a reward is perfectly predicted, dopaminergic activity remains at baseline, suggesting that zero prediction errors are not represented by a distinct dopaminergic response. This perspective is also reflected in human research using scalp-recorded event-related potentials (ERPs) 33 to investigate reward prediction error signaling 34,35 , where zero prediction errors are typically treated as the absence of a learning signal rather than as a meaningful computational state. Overall, the frontocentral feedback-related negativity and the subsequent centroparietal P300 have been associated with motivational salience, belief updating, and higher-order evaluation of outcomes 33,36,37 . Importantly, these interpretations are typically grounded in reinforcement learning accounts in which ERP amplitude is expected to vary as a function of reward prediction error 33,35,38 . We challenge this assumption by testing whether zero prediction errors are treated in a non-linear fashion, that is, eliciting larger activity than what would be expected from a linear relationship between prediction error size and neural activity. Preliminary evidence 39 

shows greater neural activity to perfectly predicted outcomes than anticipated, but it was regarded more as a nuisance in the few cases where zero prediction errors randomly occurred rather than a robust empirical finding. As such, those cases were disregarded for further analyses. Here, we provide a direct empirical test by directly manipulating zero prediction errors.

Here, we provide a first systematic investigation of zero prediction errors as potentially meaningful events in human reinforcement learning. Participants performed a task in which they repeatedly predicted rewards drawn from Gaussian distributions that varied in uncertainty. Crucially, a subset of trials was experimentally constrained such that outcomes exactly matched participants’ predictions, producing true zero prediction errors without participants having explicit knowledge of the manipulation. This design allowed us to examine whether perfectly predicted outcomes influence affective experience, subsequent belief updating, and neural feedback processing. Our approach was three-fold. First, we tested whether zero prediction errors shape positive affect beyond what can be explained by reward magnitude alone and found that momentary happiness was highest following outcomes that perfectly matched expectations. Second, using computational modeling, we asked whether zero prediction errors influence subsequent belief updating, and found that learning was best explained by a model in which zero prediction errors induce a distinct latent belief state that shapes future behavior, particularly under greater uncertainty. Third, we examined whether zero prediction errors are reflected in canonical electrophysiological signatures of feedback processing, and found that they elicited distinct reward-locked EEG responses linked to this latent zero-prediction-error belief state. Together, this work challenges the ongoing assumption that little happens when expectations are perfectly met, suggesting that zero prediction errors are a special signal that people use during learning. 

Results 

To investigate the impact of zero prediction errors on subjective well-being and learning, participants made trial-by-trial predictions about outcomes generated from a probabilistic reward distribution of varying widths (see Figure 1A, adapted from 40 and 41 ). Right after each prediction, participants were presented with the actual reward and the resulting prediction error. Crucially, only outcomes could determine reward; prediction accuracy was not incentivized. Unbeknownst to participants, 15 trials out of 80 (18.75%) in each block were altered such that the reward outcome exactly matched the prediction the participant made, producing a zero prediction error. These zero-prediction-error trials were randomly distributed throughout each block and were imposed independently of the Gaussian reward distribution. To investigate momentary subjective well-being throughout the task, participants were asked every three trials: “How happy are you at this moment?” 22 . Data were collected across two experiments (total N = 137; an online experiment, n = 97, and an EEG experiment, n = 40). Behavioral analyses were conducted first for the online experiment and then replicated in the laboratory for the EEG experiment (Supplementary Table 3-5). We present the behavioral results pooled for simplification purposes. 

People are the happiest after zero prediction errors 

To validate our measure of subjective well-being 22 , we first analyzed whether it was sensitive to aspects of the task. First, in line with previous findings 22 , subjective well-being decreased over the course of the experiment (mean ± SEM; initial 65 ± 25, final 54 ± 28, t(134) = 4.16, p < .001; Figure 1B). Furthermore, subjective well-being also captured the negative affect caused by higher levels of uncertainty 21 , as happiness was lower when rewards were more difficult to predict (Low vs. Middle: b

= −1.16, p < .001; Low vs. High: b = −2.90, p < .001). Together, these results show that our happiness measure can capture expected fluctuations of subjective well-being. ZePE model: Perfect predictions drive a separate belief state that drives learning and behavior 

How do people adapt after a zero prediction error? To test this, prediction updating was defined as the change in a participant’s next prediction relative to their current prediction, i.e., prediction on trial 

t + 1 minus prediction on trial t. As expected, larger absolute prediction errors lead to larger prediction updates ( b = 0.24, z = 14.95, p < .001), confirming that participants adjusted their beliefs in proportion to the magnitude of prediction error. Next, we tested whether zero prediction errors exerted an additional influence on updating beyond standard prediction-error magnitude. To this end, we included a regressor indexing zero-prediction-error trials in the same mixed-effects model, together with its interaction with uncertainty level. Zero-prediction-error trials were followed by larger absolute prediction updates in Low uncertainty blocks, b = 3.11, z = 7.13, p < .001, and this effect increased under greater uncertainty, as shown by significant interactions in Middle, b = 1.12, z = 3.13, p = .002, and High uncertainty blocks, b = 1.52, z = 4.29, p < .001. Together, these findings suggest that perfectly predicted outcomes influenced subsequent updating above and beyond standard prediction-error-based learning, consistent with the idea that zero prediction errors may represent a distinct psychological event that influences subsequent updating beyond the mere absence of error. Standard reinforcement learning models assume that learning is driven exclusively by prediction errors 

> 1,42

, such that larger deviations between expected and observed outcomes produce larger belief updates. We therefore began with a standard Pearce–Hall model (PH-standard) 3 in which the learning rate scales with the magnitude of recent prediction errors. Importantly, this instantiation does not explicitly account for zero prediction errors. In the PH-standard model, a zero prediction error produces no direct update to the current value estimate, because the observed outcome matches the expected outcome. However, our behavioral results showed that zero prediction errors had a particularly strong positive effect on later predictions, suggesting that perfect predictions may play an important role in shaping learning dynamics. To test this possibility, we considered two extensions of the PH-standard model. First, we implemented the PH-persist model, in which receiving a zero prediction error induces a persistent state that influences subsequent learning. Within reinforcement learning frameworks, reduced updating or ‘stickiness’ can emerge either through low learning rates, which limit the influence of new information on existing beliefs, or through an additional preservation parameter that biases behavior toward previous responses 43,44 . Building on this idea, we hypothesized that the unique nature of zero

prediction errors—both in terms of their subjective impact and their role in learning—may dynamically engage such persistence. Specifically, moments of perfect prediction may trigger a temporary bias toward maintaining current beliefs, effectively ‘locking in’ the perfect outcome. In the PH-persist model, perfect predictions therefore modify the learning process beyond the immediate trial, allowing zero prediction errors to shape future belief updates by biasing subsequent predictions toward the confirmed outcome. The degree of persistence is controlled by a free parameter, ρ, which determines how strongly zero prediction errors influence future predictions. Higher values of ρ result in stronger persistence toward the confirmed outcome, whereas lower values of ρ revert to the standard PH update with little to no persistence. Second, we tested the ZePE model, inspired by recent work showing that large prediction errors can trigger the formation of a separate latent belief state 29 . In this framework, surprising outcomes can give rise to a new state that is tracked in parallel to ongoing learning. Extending this idea, we hypothesized that the unique nature of zero prediction errors may similarly give rise to a distinct latent state. According to the ZePE model, participants are assumed to maintain two latent beliefs about the outcome distribution. One belief is updated using standard prediction errors according to the Pearce– Hall learning rule, capturing gradual belief updating following mismatches between expected and observed outcomes. The second belief specifically tracks trials in which the prediction exactly matches the outcome (zero prediction errors), reflecting a state in which the participant’s current expectation is perfectly accurate. Behavior is then generated from a weighted combination of these two belief states, controlled by a free parameter, ω, which determines the relative influence of the zero-prediction-error belief state and the standard-prediction-error-driven belief state on subsequent predictions. Values of ω closer to 1 indicate that predictions are mostly driven by the zero-prediction-error belief state, whereas values closer to 0 indicate that predictions are primarily driven by the standard prediction error–driven belief state. After validating the models through model- and parameter-recovery analyses (see Supplementary Results ), we next fitted all three models to participants’ behavior to test whether learning was best explained by standard prediction-error–driven updating (PH-standard model), persistent effects of perfect predictions (PH-persist model), or a separate belief state triggered by zero prediction errors (ZePE model). Models were fit using Bayesian hierarchical modeling (separate sequences for each participant and block), to get more stable parameter estimates, and compared using leave-one-out cross-validation (LOO), which estimates each model’s out-of-sample predictive accuracy. For the online experiment, model comparison strongly favored the ZePE model (ELPD LOO =−93,468.97), which substantially outperformed both the PH-persist model (ΔELPD = 1,109.92) and the PH-standard model (ΔELPD = 4,806.30). As shown in Figure 2C, the ZePE model received most of the LOO stacking weight (0.83), compared with 0.15 for the PH-persist model and 0.02 for the standard PH-standard model. At the participant level, the ZePE model provided the best predictive fit for most individuals (84 participants), compared with 9 participants best fit by the PH-persist model and 4 by the standard PH-standard model. Critically, the same pattern was observed in the EEG experiment . Again, the ZePE model provided the best predictive account of behavior (ELPD LOO =−67,533.91), outperforming both the PH-persist model (ΔELPD = 1,551.16) and the standard PH-standard model (ΔELPD = 5,062.62). The ZePE model received most of the model weight (0.92) and provided the best fit for all participants in the EEG dataset.

To further interrogate this mechanism, we examined the free parameter ω, which governs the influence of zero prediction errors on subsequent updating. A repeated-measures ANOVA revealed a significant effect of uncertainty on ω ( F(2,268) = 6.59, p = .002), indicating that ω varied systematically across uncertainty levels. Pairwise comparisons showed that ω was significantly higher in High compared to Low blocks ( pcorr = .003) and in Middle compared to Low blocks ( pcorr = .024), while there was no difference between Middle and High blocks ( pcorr = .444; Figure 2E). This pattern indicates that ω increases with environmental uncertainty, consistent with a progressive reliance on zero prediction errors under higher uncertainty.

Empirical behavioral support for the ZePE model 

Having identified the ZePE model as the best-fitting account of participants’ predictions, we next asked whether its core updating pattern was visible in participants’ trial-by-trial behavior. To do this, we simulated predictions using parameter values matched to those estimated from the empirical data. We compared the qualitative patterns produced by the PH-standard and ZePE models, focusing on how each model behaved around zero-prediction-error events. The left panel in Figure 2C shows example participant blocks together with the corresponding model fits. The ZePE model closely tracked the observed trial-by-trial predictions and, as shown in the right panel, captured the structure of behavioral updating around zero prediction errors. In contrast to the PH-standard model, which assumes minimal updating when prediction error is zero, the ZePE model allows zero-prediction-error events to influence subsequent beliefs. This difference is visible in the update functions: behavioral data showed a clear increase in updating around zero prediction error, and this qualitative pattern was captured by the ZePE model. We then examined this effect more directly across uncertainty levels (Figure 2D). In the behavioral data, absolute prediction updates showed a pronounced bump around zero prediction error, with larger updates on and around zero-prediction-error trials. The ZePE simulations reproduced this pattern, showing enhanced latent updating around zero prediction error across uncertainty levels. Consistent with the model results reported above, the influence of zero prediction errors became more pronounced under higher environmental uncertainty. Together, these qualitative patterns provide behavioral support for the ZePE model by showing that participants updated not only in response to classical prediction errors, but also around zero-prediction-error events. Moreover, we carried out simulations in which the mixture parameter ω was systematically varied across its full range in steps of 0.33. These simulations showed that the strength of zero-PE-related updating depended on ω: larger ω values attenuated the relationship between prediction error and update magnitude, producing a shallower prediction-error–update function (Figure 2F). Qualitatively, the behavioral data showed the same pattern: when observations were grouped into tertiles based on estimated ω values, participants with lower ω showed steeper PE-to-update relationships, whereas participants with higher ω showed shallower updating. We tested this relationship using a linear mixed-effects model with continuous ω and uncertainty level as predictors. This analysis showed that the relationship between ω and absolute prediction updates differed significantly by uncertainty level, with stronger attenuation in Middle blocks, ω × Middle: b = −4.31, p < .001, and High blocks, ω × High: 

b = −4.28, p < .001, relative to Low blocks. Thus, higher ω was associated with reduced behavioral updating, particularly under greater uncertainty, providing behavioral support for the interpretation of ω as capturing an attenuation of updating in the best-fitting ZePE model. Next, we examined whether individual differences in ω were related to task performance, indexed by absolute prediction error relative to the task mean, and whether this relationship depended on how far participants’ predictions were from the true task mean on zero-prediction-error trials. Omega relates to intolerance of uncertainty 

Intolerance of uncertainty may be especially relevant to zero prediction errors because perfectly predicted outcomes provide a momentary resolution of uncertainty. Individuals high in intolerance of uncertainty tend to experience uncertain situations as aversive and may differ in how they interpret and resolve probabilistic outcomes 45 . Consistent with this idea, recent reinforcement-learning work suggests that higher intolerance of uncertainty is associated with increased sensitivity to positive feedback and greater reliance on confirmatory evidence in changing environments 46 . We therefore examined whether individuals’ uncertainty attitudes, which were captured by the intolerance of uncertainty scale 47 , predicted individual differences in ω, while including environmental uncertainty as a within-subject factor. This analysis revealed a significant main effect of intolerance of uncertainty on ω ( b = 0.002, p = 0.03; Figure 2G), indicating that individuals who are more intolerant of uncertainty tended to place greater weight on the zero-prediction-error latent belief state, 𝐵 !"#$ . However, there was no significant interaction between intolerance of uncertainty and environmental uncertainty, indicating that this relationship was consistent across uncertainty levels. These results suggest that intolerance of uncertainty is associated with a trait-like tendency to rely more strongly on the zero-prediction-error-related latent state.

Discussion 

Our findings challenge the longstanding assumption that perfectly predicted outcomes are psychologically neutral. Across subjective affect, behavior, and electrophysiology, zero prediction errors consistently emerged as distinct and informative events. First, participants reported the highest levels of momentary happiness following outcomes that perfectly matched their expectations, with affect showing an inverted-U relationship with prediction-error magnitude that could not be explained by reward magnitude alone. Second, computational modeling showed that learning was better captured by a model in which zero prediction errors induce a distinct latent belief state that shapes subsequent behavior, rather than by standard prediction-error-driven updating. The influence of this state increased under higher environmental uncertainty and was stronger in individuals with greater intolerance of uncertainty, suggesting that perfect predictions become increasingly important when the environment is difficult to predict, particularly for those who are less comfortable with uncertainty. Finally, electrophysiological analyses revealed that zero prediction errors elicited robust and sustained reward-locked EEG responses and selectively engaged neural signals linked to the latent zero-prediction-error belief state. Critically, residual EEG activity predicted subsequent belief updating in opposite directions depending on trial type: greater neural responses were associated with attenuated updating following zero prediction errors, but enhanced updating following standard-prediction-error

We provide the first evidence that participants consistently reported the highest momentary happiness following zero prediction errors. A central implication of this finding is that perfectly predicted outcomes may be intrinsically rewarding. Importantly, participants in our task were not explicitly incentivized for prediction accuracy: monetary reward was determined by the outcome itself, rather than by how closely their prediction matched that outcome. The affective benefit of zero prediction errors therefore cannot be reduced to external reward for task accuracy. Instead, perfect predictions may be rewarding because they minimize the discrepancy between the organism’s current internal state and a desired goal state of successful prediction 54 . Previous work in gambling tasks (e.g., 22 ) and probabilistic reward-learning tasks (e.g., 21 ) has shown that expectations play a central role in happiness. Consistent with this, participants’ happiness ratings in the present study were more strongly influenced by prediction errors than by reward outcomes alone. Under standard reinforcement-learning accounts, zero prediction errors should carry little informational or affective value beyond the reward outcome itself 1,23,25 . In contrast, we found that zero-prediction-error trials were associated with enhanced subjective well-being, suggesting that perfectly predicted outcomes carry intrinsic affective value beyond reward magnitude alone. Critically, however, prior work has not directly examined the affective significance of moments in which expectations are perfectly accurate, particularly in settings where participants must actively learn and refine an internal model of the environment rather than passively observe externally determined outcomes. In this context, a zero prediction error may not only indicate a favorable outcome but also signal that the structure of the environment has been successfully understood. This interpretation resonates with work on moments of insight, where sudden gains in understanding are often accompanied by happiness and positive affect ( 55 , for review see 56 ). Such moments are also frequently characterized by a reduction in uncertainty, as a previously ambiguous situation becomes clearer and more understandable 57 . Thus, the positive affect associated with zero prediction errors may reflect a broader mechanism through which successful model formation and confirmation of environmental structure contribute to momentary well-being. A major implication of these findings concerns the role of surprise in learning. Although prediction errors are typically treated as the primary learning signal 3,58,59 , the present findings suggest that the absence of prediction error can also become informative when it is itself unexpected. In other words, zero-prediction-error events may not always be processed as simple confirmation of an existing belief. Instead, when perfect prediction is rare, they may acquire behavioral relevance and be represented as a distinct latent belief state 50 . This implies that the influence of zero prediction errors may depend on their subjective or statistical surprise. In relatively stable environments, where accurate predictions are expected, zero-prediction-error events may be adequately handled by a single-state learning process based on standard prediction-error updating. By contrast, in dynamic, volatile, or difficult decision-making environments, where exact prediction is unlikely, they may signal something more meaningful about the underlying task structure. This possibility could be tested directly by manipulating the statistical frequency of zero-prediction-error events. Similar to work showing that multiple latent beliefs can be generated dynamically as a function of surprise or environmental uncertainty 29 , future versions of the ZePE model could allow learning to shift dynamically between standard prediction-error updating and zero-prediction-error-specific latent states. The present findings also raise important questions about the neural systems through which zero prediction errors influence learning. Thus, residual EEG activity did not have a single behavioral meaning. When the outcome differed from what participants expected, stronger neural responses promoted belief change; when the outcome matched the participant’s prediction, the same signal appeared to support belief entrenchment. This stabilizing effect was stronger when omega was higher, indicating that participants who placed more weight on the zero-prediction-error latent state were more likely to translate outcome-related EEG activity into belief entrenchment rather than belief change. In this sense, omega may act as a control parameter that determines how neural responses to outcomes are used: either to update beliefs after an error, or to protect and solidify the current belief after a perfectly predicted outcome. One possibility is that omega is shaped by dopaminergic mechanisms, given the central role of dopamine in reward prediction-error signaling 23,30 . However, dopamine is unlikely to provide a complete account. Neuromodulatory systems are increasingly thought to regulate not only the size of prediction-error signals, but also how flexibly organisms update beliefs under uncertainty. For example, pupil-linked arousal, often associated with locus coeruleus–noradrenaline function, tracks trial-by-trial changes in learning rate in volatile environments, while acetylcholine and noradrenaline have been proposed to support different forms of uncertainty processing 26,60,61 . Recent work also suggests that dopamine may regulate learning dynamics or policy learning rate, rather than acting only as a scalar prediction-error signal 62 . Similar to this, omega may index a neuromodulatory tuning process that determines whether outcome-related activity promotes belief change or belief stability. This interpretation remains speculative, but provides a clear direction for future work using pharmacological manipulation, fMRI, pupillometry, or direct measures of dopaminergic and noradrenergic function. This distinction is present in Bayesian and uncertainty-sensitive accounts of learning 24,26,63–65 , and distributional reinforcement-learning models 66–68 . The association we found between intolerance of uncertainty and omega further suggests that the weighting of zero prediction errors may differ systematically across individuals. People can flexibly adjust how much they incorporate new information into their beliefs depending on uncertainty and task demands 24,27,69–71 . Yet in the present study, higher intolerance of uncertainty was associated with greater reliance on the zero-prediction-error latent state regardless of environmental uncertainty level. This pattern suggests that individuals who find uncertainty more aversive may show a more trait-like bias toward evidence that the environment is predictable, even when that evidence is not fully representative of the broader task structure. This may have implications for understanding maladaptive belief updating in anxiety and related conditions, where uncertainty is often experienced as aversive and individuals may have difficulty learning the causal structure of changing environments 72–74 . Future work could test whether increased reliance on zero-prediction-error states reflects an adaptive strategy for reducing uncertainty, or whether it contributes to inflexible belief entrenchment when environments require updating. Several limitations should be noted. First, although zero prediction errors influenced both momentary happiness and subsequent belief updating, the present study does not establish a causal link between affect and learning. Positive affect following zero prediction errors may facilitate later belief entrenchment or memory encoding, consistent with evidence that positive prediction-error events can strengthen memory formation 75 . However, affective responses are rarely incorporated into standard reinforcement-learning models, and future work should test whether the subjective value of accurate prediction directly contributes to learning, memory, or model stability. Taken together, these findings suggest that successful predictions are an essential part of learning, helping to stabilize beliefs and reduce uncertainty about the world. 

Methods (STAR) 

Online experiment 

Participants. One hundred and one individuals carried out the number generation task. Four participants were removed due to no variance in their happiness ratings, leaving a final sample of 97 participants (48 females, 47 males, 2 NA) between the ages of 18 and 45 in Experiment 1. All participants were recruited from the United States via Prolific, spoke fluent English, and provided written informed consent. The study was approved by the Psychology Research Ethics Committee of Leiden University (CEP: M.L. Vives Moya-V1-5664). 

Experimental Task. Participants performed a number generation task designed to assess learning of reward distribution means. The task was programmed in PsychoPy, hosted online via Pavlovia, and distributed through Prolific. All participants completed the study remotely on their own desktop computers. Each participant completed three blocks of 80 trials. In each block, rewards were drawn from a Gaussian distribution that varied in both precision (standard deviation [SD]: low = 10, medium = 20, high = 25; block order counterbalanced across participants) and expected value (mean randomly drawn from 40 to 60). Each trial consisted of several phases. First, a prediction screen appeared, displaying a vertical bar and a movable slider (range: 0–100). Participants used the mouse to indicate their prediction of the upcoming reward. After confirming their response, the selected value remained

EEG acquisition. EEG was recorded using a BioSemi ActiveTwo system (BioSemi) with 32 Ag–AgCl electrodes arranged according to the international 10–20 system. To monitor ocular activity, additional electrodes were placed above and below the left eye (vertical electrooculogram [EOG]) and on the outer canthi of both eyes (horizontal EOG). Data were continuously recorded at 512 Hz. Electrode impedance was kept within acceptable limits according to BioSemi’s active electrode system specifications. 

EEG preprocessing. EEG analyses were conducted using the MNE-Python package 76 . The data were first re-referenced to the average of the left and right mastoid electrodes, and then band-pass filtered between 1 Hz and 30 Hz using a FIR filter with a 'firwin' design. Independent component analysis (ICA) was used to remove artifacts from the EEG data. First, we decomposed the continuous EEG signal into 15 components using the Infomax algorithm implemented in MNE. Next, we applied automatic component classification using MNE’s label_components function, which labels components based on their spatial and temporal characteristics. Components associated with ocular artifacts (e.g., blinks), cardiac signals, and muscle activity were automatically identified and excluded from the data. The cleaned signal was then segmented into epochs time-locked to reward onset, spanning from –200 ms to +1000 ms. These epochs were baseline-corrected by subtracting the mean voltage in the 200 ms window prior to reward onset. Epochs containing large residual artifacts were excluded from further analysis using a peak-to-peak amplitude threshold of 250 μV. A total of 52 bad epochs were dropped across all participants, leaving 99.07% clean trials for analysis. 

EEG analysis. EEG data were analyzed using a mass univariate regression approach. For each participant, EEG amplitude at every electrode and timepoint relative to reward onset was regressed onto a design matrix containing task-relevant regressors of interest alongside nuisance covariates. Regressors of interest varied across models and included trial-wise estimates of absolute prediction error (z-scored), trial type (0 = standard trial, 1 = zero-prediction-error trial), and model-derived latent prediction error signals (e.g., 𝑜 & − 𝐵 &

Group-level statistics were performed using one-sample t-tests on these coefficients across participants, resulting in spatiotemporal maps of t-statistics for each regressor of interest. Cluster-level statistics were computed by summing t-values within each cluster. The significance of observed clusters was assessed against a null distribution generated via 2000 permutations using sign-flipping across participants. Clusters with p-values below α = .05 were considered significant.

Model fitting. Model parameters were estimated using hierarchical Bayesian inference implemented in Python with the PyMC library 81 . For each model, subject-level parameters were drawn from group-level distributions, allowing partial pooling across participants while preserving individual differences 

> 82

. Models were fitted separately for the online and EEG experiments, such that partial pooling occurred within each experiment and group-level estimates were experiment specific. Subject-specific parameters were estimated separately for each level of environmental uncertainty. Parameters constrained to the unit interval, including 𝛼 . and 𝛾 , and, where applicable, 𝜌 and 𝜔 were estimated on the unconstrained real line using hierarchical normal distributions. For each uncertainty level, subject-level raw parameters were drawn from a group-level normal distribution, with normal priors on the group means and half-normal priors on the group standard deviations. Raw parameters were then mapped to the interval (0,1) using a logistic transformation. Observation-noise parameters were modeled hierarchically on the log scale and exponentiated to ensure positivity. Group-level parameters defined the population means and standard deviations of the corresponding subject-level distributions. Posterior inference was performed using Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling with the No-U-Turn Sampler implemented in PyMC. For each model, we ran four independent chains with 1,000 tuning iterations followed by 1,000 sampling iterations per chain. The target acceptance rate was set to 0.95 to improve sampling stability. Simulation analysis. To visualize the behavioral consequences of the fitted learning models, we performed a simulation analysis using the empirical group-mean parameter estimates obtained from the hierarchical Bayesian model fits. We simulated trial-by-trial predictions and latent belief states from the standard Pearce–Hall model and the ZePE model. For the PH-standard model, simulations used 

𝛼 . = 0.17 , 𝛾 = 0.06 , and 𝜎 = 18 . For the ZePE model, simulations used 𝛼 . = 0.72 , 𝛾 = 0.21 ,

𝜔 = 0.52 , and 𝜎 = 13 . For each model, we generated 1,000 simulated blocks for each level of environmental uncertainty, with feedback standard deviations of 10, 20, and 25. Each block contained 80 trials, with zero-prediction-error trials occurring on 18.75% of trials, matching the empirical task design. For each simulation, the environmental mean was sampled uniformly between 40 and 60 and was held constant across models, allowing direct comparison of the two models under matched task conditions. We then extracted signed and absolute prediction errors, observed prediction updates, and latent belief-state updates from the simulated trial sequences. Observed updates were defined as the change in sampled prediction from trial t to trial t+1 , whereas latent updates were defined as the corresponding change in the model’s internal belief state. 

Parameter recovery. To assess whether model parameters could be reliably recovered from behavioral data, we conducted parameter recovery analyses for the three models considered in the main analyses (PH, PH-persist, and ZePE model). For each model, simulated datasets were generated using the same task structure as in the online experiment (240 trials per dataset). On each simulation, model parameters were sampled from uniform distributions spanning the parameter ranges used during model fitting (e.g., 𝛼 ., 𝛾 ∈ [0,1], 𝜌 ∈ [0,2], 𝜔 ∈ [0,1], and 𝜎 ∈ [1,12 ]). Outcome values were sampled from a Gaussian distribution with mean 60 and standard deviation 20, matching the approximate distribution of lottery outcomes in the task. In the simulation, 18.75% of trials were randomly selected to produce a zero prediction error, paralleling the behavioral study. Using these parameters, we simulated trial-by-trial predictions according to the generative model. Simulated behavioral data were then refit using the same model and fitting procedure as used for the empirical data. This procedure was repeated for 300 simulated datasets per model. Parameter recovery was assessed by computing the correlation between the true generative parameters and the parameters recovered by the fitting procedure. In addition, we computed the bias (mean difference between recovered and true parameters) and root-mean-square error (RMSE) for each parameter. Results of the parameter recovery analyses indicated that the key model parameters ( 𝛼 ., 𝛾 , and 𝜎 , as well as 𝜌 

for PH-persist and 𝜔 for the ZePE model) were reliably recovered across simulations (see 

Supplementary Results ). 

Model recovery. To assess whether the candidate models could be dissociated, we conducted a model recovery analysis for the three models entered into the main comparison (PH, PH-persist, and ZePE model; Wilson & Collins, 2019). Synthetic datasets were generated from each model using parameter values sampled from the admissible parameter ranges and the same task structure as in the experiment (240 trials per dataset). In total, 200 datasets were simulated, split evenly across the three generative models. Each simulated dataset was then fit with all candidate models, and model identity was recovered using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC).
