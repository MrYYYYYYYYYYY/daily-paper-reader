## ABSTRACT 

The seminal reward prediction error account of dopamine has been highly successful, but faces 1

several key challenges. Most notable are the difficulty of learning multiple rewards simultaneously, 2

inefficient on-policy learning, and accounting for the heterogeneous striatal responses observed 3

across and within striatal targets. Here we address these issues with a normative entropy-regularised 4

reinforcement-learning framework. We propose that dopamine optimises not just cumulative rewards, 5

but a reward value function augmented by a penalty for deviating from a default behavioural policy. 6

In simulations, this off-policy formulation provides a principled solution to composing multiple 7

reward values, avoids the interference and unintended unlearning seen in multi-objective on-policy 8

methods when priorities change, and adapts more efficiently than standard alternatives in environments 9

with non-stationary rewards. More broadly, the framework offers a unified account of dopamine 10 

heterogeneity between and within striatal targets, including a normative way to understand why 11 

aversive and action prediction errors may coexist in the tail of the striatum. Together, these results 12 

suggest that dopamine-mediated learning may be better captured by prediction errors in composable, 13 

entropy-regularised value functions than by a single broadcast prediction error, and offer testable 14 

predictions for future experiments. 15 

## 1 Introduction 16 

Almost three decades ago, Schultz et al. [1] proposed that midbrain dopamine (DA) neuron phasic activity encodes 17 

temporal difference errors (TDEs). This fundamental idea, leveraging the temporal difference (TD) learning algorithm 18 

[2], suggested the brain could assign credit in terms of expected future reward using temporally successive predictions. 19 

Numerous experiments have since substantiated this relationship within the TD reinforcement learning (TDRL) 20 

framework [ 3, 4, 5 , 6, 7 ]. A core motivation was that engineered systems employ similar algorithms to optimise actions 21 

in complex environments, mirroring challenges faced by animals [ 1]. However, three key challenges currently impede 22 

TDRL research from fully realising this core motivation. 23 

The first challenge arises because typical experiments and computational models, utilising single-attribute rewards 24 

(e.g., juice) and monolithic value functions, inadequately capture the multi-objective nature of challenges animals face 25 

in real life. Animals must constantly satisfy distinct, often conflicting objectives under changing priorities, a kind 26 

of non-stationary reward landscape. This necessitates either multiple value functions [ 8, 9] or alternative efficient 27 

representations like the successor representation [ 10, 11 , 12], as standard RL approaches struggle with instant revaluation 28 

under non-stationary rewards [ 8 , 9 , 13 ]. This need for fast adaptation to multiple objectives (often encoded by different 29 

rewarding attributes), under shifting priorities, is ubiquitous in homeostasis [ 14, 15 , 16 ] and extends to human cognitive 30 

tasks [ 17 ]. Neural evidence further suggests dopaminergic circuits projecting to different targets may encode multiple 31 

value functions corresponding to various reward modalities (e.g., food, juice, water [ 18 , 19 , 20 , 21 , 22 ]), valence [ 23], 32 

substance type [ 24 ], or even abstract features and contexts [ 25 , 26, 27 ], yet this multiplicity is not captured by standard 33 

TDRL. 34

The second challenge concerns the TD-learning rule used by Schultz et al. [1] , which learns values under the current 35 

behavioural policy (on-policy algorithms), rather than under an optimal policy (off-policy algorithms). The result is that 36 

the learned values estimate future returns assuming continued use of the current, often suboptimal, policy. While a core 37 

motivation was to learn optimal actions [ 1 ], on-policy algorithms characteristically learn suboptimal values under an 38 

often-exploring, suboptimal policy. This compromise, well-noted in traditional single-objective RL [ 28 ] and usually 39 

dealt with by heuristically tuning exploratory noise, becomes particularly problematic in multi-objective RL, where the 40 

behavioural policy can change drastically with shifts in needs or non-stationarity [8]. 41 

The third challenge stems from widespread dopamine heterogeneity, which calls into question the physiological basis of 42 

a single, broadcasted scalar reward prediction error (RPE). While a final scalar variable is computationally necessary 43 

to guide choice, the notion of a monolithic RPE signal is increasingly at odds with observations of diverse dopamine 44 

responses, both between and within striatal subregions. Extending the classical model [ 1] to multiple objectives [ 9]45 

only partially explains this diversity (e.g., between different DA targets). For instance, within-target heterogeneity in 46 

regions like the tail of the striatum (TS) remains difficult to reconcile, as evidence points to a mix of threat and action 47 

prediction errors [23, 29, 30, 31, 32] that do not neatly integrate into the standard TDRL framework. 48 

While distinct, the first two challenges—the need for multi-objective learning and the pitfalls of on-policy methods— 49 

converge on a single, fundamental question of optimal composition of multiple values. The brain clearly possesses 50 

mechanisms for learning multiple values, but how can they be reliably combined to produce a coherent and optimal 51 

policy? Ideally, this “recipe” for combining values should satisfy two key properties: optimality and compositionality. 52 

Optimality, the bedrock of modern reinforcement learning [ 33], simply means choosing the best possible action [ 34 ]. 53 

Compositionality, conversely, is the formal principle of constructing solutions to complex problems from a set of 54 

modular components, for instance by allowing a multi-attribute task to be represented by a basis set of value functions 55 

tuned to individual reward dimensions. Crucially, this structure allows policies for novel priority landscapes to be 56 

constructed by flexibly recombining these components, obviating the need to learn each new configuration de novo. 57 

As noted previously [ 7], the choice of learning algorithm is not a mere technicality but a foundational choice dictating 58 

the system’s computational objectives. Recent proposals often overlook this, foregoing either optimality [ 9] or 59 

composability [ 8]. On-policy methods (e.g., SARSA), for instance, suffer from learning interference, especially under 60 

shifting contextual priorities. When the current context prioritises one reward, the agent’s policy becomes biased; 61 

because learning is tied to this policy, the valuation of alternative rewards is not learned in isolation but is corrupted 62 

by being evaluated through the lens of the current trajectory. In contrast, off-policy methods (e.g., Q-learning) fail 63 

because of the non-linearity of the Bellman optimality ( max ) operator, which is not additive. This creates an unrealistic 64 

assumption: the agent is treated as perfectly rational during valuation (identifying the single best future action) yet as 65 

boundedly rational during action selection (making stochastic choices). This inconsistency between ideal valuation and 66 

bounded action selection corrupts the composition of different reward values. 67 

To resolve this, we adopt a normative framework from control engineering [ 35 , 36 , 34 , 37 ], broadly termed linear RL 68 

[38 ]. We propose that the dopamine system’s objective is not merely to optimise cumulative reward, but to optimise 69 

returns augmented by a penalty for deviating from a default policy. This single modification provides a principled 70 

solution to the optimal composition problem [ 39 ]. By enforcing a consistent assumption of bounded rationality 71 

throughout both valuation and action selection, it resolves the paradoxical logic of standard algorithms, allowing 72 

multiple values to be robustly combined. 73 

Remarkably, the same principle that ensures optimal composition provides a natural framework for understanding 74 

dopamine heterogeneity. The framework’s parallel architecture for outcome-specific prediction errors explains between-75 

target dopamine heterogeneity [ 9, 40 ] and supports efficient learning across multiple rewards. We substantiate these 76 

claims in our Results, which use a didactic example and simulations to demonstrate these normative advantages 77 

across multi-objective tasks. Lastly, we discuss how this compositional logic can be extended to explain within-target 78 

heterogeneity, formally reconciling threat and action prediction errors in the TS. 79

unintended revaluation and even unlearning of values for other modalities (Fig. 2E). The unstable value estimates 143 

demonstrate how an on-policy agent struggles to adapt: its learned value for ‘Juice 1’ is also inadvertently altered while 144 

it pursues ‘Juice 2’, preventing a truly flexible switch when the rules change. In contrast, our off-policy multi-objective 145 

(MO) soft Q-learning framework effectively mitigates this interference, as the valuation of the next state is dependent 146 

on the default policy separate from the current behavioural policy. This ensures stable learning of all value components 147

2.3 Efficient learning and off-policy fast adaptation 149 

Efficient adaptation to non-stationary rewards is a hallmark of intelligence. While full model-based RL offers a solution, 150 

it is computationally costly. Part model-free solutions rely on either (i) composing independent value functions for 151 

different (pre-defined) reward types, as discussed above [ 8, 9], or (ii) learning efficient representations such as the 152 

successor representation (SR) [10], which allow rapid revaluation of policies when reward functions change. 153 

Crucially, in the case of on-policy model-free algorithms, Millidge et al. [9] show that a combination of TD(0) learning 154 

rules for each of the reward types is akin to a compressed SR with rewards tuned only to relevant dimensions. An 155 

equivalent relationship in off-policy algorithms is lacking. The default representation (DR), an off-policy counterpart to 156 

the SR derived from linear MDPs [38, 36, 34], overcomes the on-policy limitations of the SR. 157

## 3 Discussion 180 

In this work, we address the fundamental challenge of reliably optimising multiple objectives in reinforcement 181 

learning and propose a model that outperforms existing multi-objective methods. This invites a reconsideration of 182 

the dopaminergic system’s computational objective, shifting from the classical view of cumulative discounted reward 183 

maximisation [ 1] towards optimising returns augmented by a KL penalty for deviations of the behavioural policy from a 184 

default policy. This reframing not only yields distinct functional advantages but also generates novel testable predictions 185 

in efficient and stable learning. In doing so, we also then discuss how the same framework can be instantiated to address 186 

conflicting views of TS-projecting dopamine in threat prediction errors (TPEs) and action prediction errors (APEs) 187 

within the temporal difference reinforcement learning framework. 188 

This approach was designed to directly address several limitations of the standard model, specifically the challenge of 189 

flexibly pursuing multiple, often conflicting, rewards without the learning process becoming unstable or inefficient—a 190 

known issue for classic on-policy methods. Our model’s core innovation is to augment the classic reward prediction 191 

error with a regularisation term that imposes a ‘cost’ for deviating from a default policy. 192 

At an algorithmic level, this work addresses how to ensure (often individually optimal) value functions cooperate 193 

effectively to drive reliable behaviour, despite competing for control. This contrasts with “delegation” approaches 194 

[55, 56, 57 ], where only one value function controls actions at any timestep, thus avoiding this problem. Regarding 195 

our optimal composition results, previous multi-objective TD(0) or SARSA approaches that scale on-policy prediction 196 

errors with weights (e.g., state-dependent RPE modulation [ 9, 58 ] or feature-specific weight updates [ 26 , 40]) may 197 

slightly ameliorate unreliable learning or policy interference, but do not resolve it (supplementary Fig. S3). This 198 

excludes trivial conditions where only one value component is active, preventing interference. A better alternative for 199 

these models might be to use importance sampling, which would ensure off-policy learning. 200 

Off-policy learning is a prominent theme in this work, as it demonstrably improves performance over on-policy 201 

multi-objective RL algorithms and the successor representation, while also relating directly to its off-policy counterpart 202 

[38]. The brain may implement off-policy learning for several reasons: First, it prevents interference and unlearning 203 

between multiple values amidst changing priorities. Second, it facilitates learning amidst motor noise and competition 204 

from distributed control systems, like the motor cortex and cerebellum [ 59 , 60 ]. Third, on-policy algorithms, such as 205 

the successor representation, exhibit strong policy dependence where goal information contaminates the state map, 206 

hindering flexible transfer [ 54 , 61 ], a problem solved by off-policy alternatives [ 38 ]. Fourth, the ability of episodic 207 

memories to utilise cached values [ 62 , 63 ] or stale behavioural data for performance improvements points towards an 208 

underlying off-policy mechanism, akin to its necessity in deep Q-learning’s episodic replay buffers [ 64]. However, 209 

finding strong neural and behavioural evidence for interference and unintended unlearning between two or more value 210 

systems (for different rewards) under changing priorities in a two-step task similar to Fig. 2D-F, would falsify our 211 

hypothesis of phasic dopamine performing off-policy multi-objective RL and find evidence for on-policy multi-objective 212 

RL (e.g. [9]). Rapid change in priorities could be potentially implemented as a task rule that needs to be inferred. 213 

Our framework synthesises several threads of the evolving dopamine story, which has progressively expanded from a 214 

simple scalar reward signal [ 1] to a multifaceted control signal. Our results (Fig. 3C) also highlight the behavioural 215 

inefficiencies of the SR model in replanning after overtraining [ 54]. Indeed, SR models of dopamine often need to treat 216 

reward as a feature to explain RPEs as a form of state prediction error [ 12], as Lee et al. [40] shows they otherwise fail 217 

to consistently respond to rewards. This highlights the benefit of outcome-specific models such as ours [and that of 9], 218 

which efficiently achieve values comparable to an SR/DR model [10, 38] when tuned to a subset of reward types. 219 

In terms of limitations; first, though broadly applicable to decision-making under changing priorities, when applied 220 

to homeostatic priorities, it cannot explain physiological state-dependent modulation of prediction errors [ 65]. This 221 

is a limitation common to all multi-objective RL approaches (e.g., those by Dulberg et al. [8] , vanilla version of 222 

Millidge et al. [9] and ours), which learn equally from all reward types, at all times. Second, despite its advantages, 223 

soft maximum composition optimises an objective different from a simple weighted sum of utilities. While this is 224 

not necessarily an issue for modelling homeostasis, where value representation is debated [ 15 , 66 , 8], and may even 225 

account for inhibitory effects of irrelevant drives [ 15 , 67], we find it can fit poorly to human behaviour when participants 226 

explicitly maximise weighted sums of utilities or show generalisation in task structure rather than in values [ 17 ] (see 227 

supplementary Fig. S5). Third, while there are compelling reasons for the brain to implement off-policy learning 228 

[59, 60 , 32 ], some early work [ 68 , 69 ] suggested phasic dopamine implements on-policy algorithms like SARSA. 229 

However, those experiments involved overtrained monkeys where no learning occurred, making observed TD error 230 

signals potentially epiphenomenal. Our framework could partly explain these differences in action values as the KL 231 

divergence from the overtrained default policy (APEs). Furthermore, the observed use of cached values in computing 232 

TDEs [62, 63] better aligns with off-policy rather than on-policy TD learning. 233
