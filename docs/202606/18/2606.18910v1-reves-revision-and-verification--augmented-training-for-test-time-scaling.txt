## Abstract 

Test-time scaling via sequential revision has emerged as a powerful paradigm for enhancing Large Language Model (LLM) reasoning. However, standard post-training methods primarily optimize single-shot objectives, creating a fun-damental misalignment with multi-step inference dynamics. While recent work treats this as multi-turn reinforcement learning (RL), conventional approaches optimize over the multi-step trajectories directly, failing to further exploit the high-quality mistakes in intermediate steps that model can learn from correct-ing them. We propose a two-stage iterative framework that alternates between online data/prompt augmentation and policy optimization. By converting the intermediate steps (“near-miss” answers) in the successful recovery trajectories into decoupled revision and verification prompts, our approach concentrates training on both effective answer transformation and error identification. This approach enables efficient off-policy data generation and reduces the computa-tional overhead of long-horizon sampling compared to standard multi-turn RL. On LiveCodeBench, using publicly available test cases as feedback, we observe gains of +6.5 points over the RL baseline and +4.0 points over standard multi-turn training. Beyond coding, our approach matches the previously reported SOTA result on circle packing while using the smallest base model (4B) and far fewer rollouts than the much larger evolutionary search systems. Math results under ground-truth verification further confirm improved correction ability. It also gen-eralizes to out-of-distribution constraint-satisfaction puzzles such as n_queens and mini_sudoku, where correctness is defined entirely by problem constraints. Code is available at https://github.com/yxliu02/REVES.git .

## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) deployed in challenging settings rarely produce a correct answer on the first attempt; real-world workflows routinely loop back with feedback. A growing family of test-time scaling (TTS) algorithms formalizes this pattern, including sequential revision (Madaan et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023), tree search (Inoue et al., 2025), evolutionary refinement (Lee et al., 2025b), and best-of-N with a verifier (Chow et al., 2025). They all share a single primitive: given a problem, a previous attempt, and feedback, produce an improved response. This raises a basic question: can we design a post-training algorithm that explicitly improves a model’s test-time sequential revision capability? We focus on sequential revision (SR) because gains on SR transfer to the rest of the family: every revision-using TTS algorithm eventually calls the policy on revise-from-prior-attempt inputs. SR is also a strong TTS algorithm in its own right, matching or surpassing tree search and evolutionary refinement on planning tasks (Figure 2). Theorem 3.1 shows that increasing the policy’s one-step revision value on SR-induced inputs also raises Jϕ for every revision-using TTS algorithm that covers SR’s revision inputs, up to small drift terms.

How, then, do we post-train an LLM to optimize the SR objective JϕSR ? Standard post-training, RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022), RLVR, and GRPO (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2025), optimizes a single-shot expected reward, which is fundamentally misaligned with the multi-step nature of test-time deployment. The natural fix is multi-turn RL on revision rollouts, but trajectory-level credit broadcasts assign path-dependent credit. Take a wrong, wrong, correct rollout: every per-turn log-probability gradient receives the same positive credit, including the wrong intermediates. Per rollout this credit is biased, and only cancels in expectation across many rollouts. We propose REVES, a two-stage iterative framework that targets the SR objective JϕSR directly through its per-state structure. We show that JϕSR decomposes exactly into a weighted sum of per-state one-step recovery probabilities along visited rollouts (Lemma 4.1), exposing single-state, single-turn gradients with no horizon credit assignment. REVES is an offline realization of this per-state training signal: each epoch, it runs SR rollouts under the current policy, retains those that succeed within budget, converts the intermediate (“near-miss”) answers into decoupled revision and verification prompts, and trains with standard single-turn RL on the augmented prompt set; the next epoch refreshes the augmentation. This concentrates training on per-state recovery and avoids both the bias of trajectory broadcasts and the cost of long-horizon online sampling. Empirically, on LiveCodeBench with public test cases as feedback, REVES achieves +6.5 points over the single-shot RL baseline and +4.0 points over standard multi-turn training. On the circle packing benchmark, a Qwen3-4B base trained with REVES matches the previously reported best results from much larger evolutionary search systems built on Gemini-2.0 Pro/Flash and Qwen3-8B. Math benchmarks (MATH500, AIME24/25) show consistent improvements under both ground-truth and self-confidence stopping, and the trained policies generalize to out-of-distribution constraint-satisfaction puzzles (n_queens, mini_sudoku). Beyond standalone SR, REVES improves every revision-using TTS algorithm we tested (MCTS, AB-MCTS variants, Mind Evolution), in line with the conditional bound in Theorem 3.1. Our contributions are threefold: 1. Conceptual. We formulate test-time scaling as a meta-RL problem and show that im-proving sequential revision also improves any revision-using TTS algorithm that covers SR’s revision inputs (Theorem 3.1). 2. Methodological. We exactly decompose JϕSR into per-state one-step recovery probabilities (Lemma 4.1) and propose REVES, a two-stage framework that recasts intermediate near-miss answers as decoupled revision and verification prompts trained with single-turn RL, avoiding the path-dependent credit assignment of multi-turn RL. 3. Empirical. REVES delivers consistent test-time gains across math, coding, out-of-distribution puzzles, and circle packing, and improves every revision-using TTS al-gorithm we evaluated. 

## 2 Related Work 

Test-time-aware post-training. A growing line of work shows the importance of aligning the post-training objective with the intended test-time inference strategy. For parallel-sampling families, this includes optimizing pass@ k (Chen et al., 2025; Walder & Karkhanis, 2025; Tang et al., 2025) when deployment uses best-of-N, majority voting (Wang et al., 2023), or learned verifiers (Chow et al., 2025). Our deployment is structurally different: the test-time strategies we care about, including sequential revision, MCTS variants, and Mind Evolution, are all revision-using algorithms whose calls take as input a previous attempt together with its feedback. Aligning training with this inference family therefore requires a different objective. 

Training sequential revision capability. Closer to our setting, several prior methods ex-plicitly train the policy to revise, including supervised and preference-based approaches (Qu et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2025), RL-based approaches (Kumar et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2025; 2Lee et al., 2025a), and multi-turn RL methods that incorporate per-turn natural-language or numerical critiques (Jain et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025b;a; Zhang et al., 2026). Our work shares the broader goal of strengthening the model’s revision capability, but differs in framing: we primarily focus on how to align training with the test-time objective of a revision-using inference strategy. 

Weakness-driven data synthesis and guided exploration. SwS (Liang et al., 2025) synthe-sizes new problems targeting the model’s weaknesses using strong external teacher models, and POPE (Qu et al., 2026) uses privileged oracle hints to guide on-policy exploration on hard problems. These methods are orthogonal to ours: they target single-shot pass@1, whereas REVES targets a fundamentally different test-time objective. 

## 3 Test-Time Scaling as a Meta-RL Problem 

Setup and two motivating examples. We fix notation used throughout the paper. Denote by X the distribution over input problems, Y the answer space, and r⋆ : X × Y → [0, 1 ]

the ground-truth reward, where r⋆(x, y) = 1 indicating a fully correct answer. We focus on the verifiable-reward regime where r⋆ is computable during both training and at test time, e.g., public test-case execution for code, and constraint satisfaction for puzzles. When r⋆ is unavailable at test time, a surrogate stopping rule replaces it (Section 4). We model the LLM as a policy πθ in RL parameterized by θ. At deployment, an LLM rarely operates by a single call to πθ ; instead a test-time scaling (TTS) algorithm ϕ orchestrates up to K policy calls and extracts a final answer ˆy ∈ Y . Two canonical examples bracket the design space: Best-of- N(BoN) (Chow et al., 2025) draws N candidates in parallel from πθ (· | x) and selects ˆy via an external scorer; every call uses the same original prompt. Sequential Revision (SR) (Madaan et al., 2023) generates an initial response y1 ∼ πθ (· | x) from prompt x, and for t = 2, . . . , K

produces a revision yt ∼ πθ (· | x, yt−1, ft−1) conditioned on the previous attempt yt−1 and a feedback signal ft−1 (e.g., compiler error messages in code generation), halting at the first correct response. Detailed pseudocode is in Protocol 1 of Appendix A. 

General formulation: the TTS-induced decision process. Pairing any TTS algorithm ϕ

with πθ induces a decision process with prompt-state space S and response space Y. At each step t, the policy emits a response yt ∼ πθ (· | st), and ϕ constructs the next prompt via a transition kernel Pϕ(st+1 | s1: t, y1: t). The initial state is s1 = x. Note that the policy itself remains Markov in the current state (at every step, πθ conditions only on the local prompt st); the history dependence is in the kernel, because ϕ may branch from any earlier response. We focus on the family ΦR of TTS algorithms that issue at least one revision call on a prior attempt; this includes Sequential Revision and adaptive tree search such as AB-MCTS (Inoue et al., 2025). For such algorithms a revision-step prompt has the form 

st+1 = ( x, yit , fit ), it ∈ { 1, . . . , t}, where the prior step it is chosen by ϕ: for SR, it = t (always the most recent response), so st+1 depends only on (st, yt) and the induced process is a standard MDP; for tree-search algorithms such as AB-MCTS, it may be any earlier response that ϕ’s search policy decides to branch from, and the kernel is genuinely history-dependent. The procedure runs until a random stopping time τ := K ∧ min {t : r⋆(x, yt) = 1} and outputs ˆy = yτ ; that is, the procedure either early-stops once a verified-correct response is produced, or exhausts the budget K . Running πθ inside ϕ yields the TTS-induced objective 

Jϕ(θ) = Ex∼X , (s1: τ ,y1: τ )∼(πθ ,ϕ)[r⋆(x, yτ )] ,to be contrasted with the standard single-shot objective 

JOneShot (θ) = Ex∼X , y∼πθ (·| x)[r⋆(x, y)] ,which is what RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022), RLVR, and GRPO (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2025) optimize. Optimizing Jϕ rather than JOneShot is a meta-RL problem (Figure 1): the policy must adapt across the distribution of multi-step contexts that ϕ generates, not just answer the original question. Our goal is to improve Jϕ for every TTS algorithm ϕ ∈ ΦR.This raises an important question: does there exist a training target Jϕtrain whose optimization provably improves Jϕ for every ϕ ∈ ΦR? From recovery target to algorithm. An ideal on-policy implementation of Lemma 4.1 draws z ∼ ρθ by running SR and runs single-turn RL at each z. Three design choices turn this into the algorithm we use. 

(1) Verification prompts alongside revision data. At test time the verifier is typically unavailable. We therefore include both revision prompts and verification prompts (asking the policy to judge whether an intermediate response is correct) in the augmented data, so the trained policy can self-stop at deployment. The split of gains across the two prompt types is in Table 5. 

(2) Offline generation. An on-policy estimator of ∇JϕSR would need fresh SR rollouts every gradient step, which is intrinsically serial and prohibitively slow. We instead generate visited states once per epoch in Stage I, reuse them through Stage II, and refresh at the next epoch. 

(3) Successful-trajectory filtering. For a far-from-optimal base policy, online samples at states from failed rollouts rarely produce a verifier-accepted recovery and waste tokens. We restrict Stage I to states on trajectories that succeed within budget K, where at least one recovery target exists by construction; the filter loosens as πθ improves.

5.2 Ablation Study The role of revision and verification prompts. As shown in Table 5, we investigate the roles of revision and verification prompts in sequential revision training. Qwen-2.5-7B-RevisionOnly augments RL-generated data using only revision prompts, while Qwen-2.5-7B-VerificationOnly augments data using only verification prompts. Qwen-2.5-7B-REVES combines both prompts during data augmentation. Results under Oracle-4 indicate that revision prompts primarily improve sequential revision capability , whereas verification prompts do not directly enhance revision but instead train the model to assess solution validity. To isolate the effect of verification prompts, we evaluate confidence calibration using Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (AUROC) on AIME25. For each problem, we sample multiple model responses, label them as correct or incorrect based on the final answer, and compute the probability that a randomly sampled correct response receives a higher TailConfidence score than a randomly sampled incorrect one. The final AUROC is obtained by averaging over problems with sufficient correct samples. Under this evaluation, Qwen-2.5-7B-REVES achieves a higher AUROC (74.1%) than RevisionOnly (72.1%), demon-strating that verification prompts improve confidence calibration. Thus, combining revision and verification prompts yields consistent gains by jointly enhancing revision capability and confidence estimation. 

Necessity of continual data augmentation. As shown in Figure 6 (right), augmenting data only at the initial epoch leads to substantially worse performance than continuous aug-mentation. This is because once the model stops making earlier mistakes, stale augmented data no longer provides informative learning signals, whereas regenerating data each epoch keeps the supervision aligned with the model’s current failure modes. 

Effect of augmentation budget. We study the effect of the augmentation budget on training performance. As shown in Figure 6 (left), increasing the augmentation budget consistently improves performance, indicating that our method exhibits strong scaling behavior with respect to the augmentation budget. ## 6 Conclusion 

We show that standard single-shot RL objectives are misaligned with test-time sequen-tial revision, and propose REVES , which pairs RL with continual, mistake-driven data augmentation so the model learns both to revise incorrect solutions and to identify errors. Augmentation must be continual, since static data becomes uninformative once mistakes disappear. Across math, coding, and out-of-distribution tasks, REVES improves test-time sequential-revision performance over the single-shot RL and multi-turn baselines under fixed budgets, and, as predicted by our transfer analysis, these gains further carry over to other revision-using test-time algorithms. ## B Limitation and Future Direction 

Limitation. REVES requires a ground-truth verifier during training. Math, coding, puzzle, and circle-packing benchmarks all admit deterministic verifiers, so REVES applies directly. For tasks without such verifiers (free-form QA, creative writing, RLHF-style preference learning), REVES does not directly extend; the verification prompts trained alongside revision data can be repurposed for self-verification at test time, but their reliability depends on the policy’s own calibration. 

Future direction. The verifier requirement points to a natural class of problems where REVES is especially well suited: scientific tasks whose objectives admit a clear, automated reward. Examples include solving partial differential equations (PDEs), where residual norms or analytic checks serve as verifiers; symbolic regression, where fit and complexity supply a numeric score; theorem proving, where a proof checker provides binary feedback; and discovery-style optimization tasks like circle packing (Appendix H), where the verifier is the geometric solver itself. In all of these settings, the cost of producing the verifier signal is offset by an essentially unlimited supply of training problems and a precise notion of correctness, both of which line up well with REVES’s reliance on per-state one-step recovery as the gradient signal. Scaling REVES to such scientific-discovery domains is a natural next step.

REVES (Ours). Our method builds on top of the same training framework as the baselines. The key difference lies in the data augmentation procedure. ## F Method Details: Multi-Turn Comparison, Efficiency, and Test-Time Sensitivity 

F.1 Comparison with Naive Multi-Turn RL 

A natural alternative to our offline scheme is online sequential revision with standard multi-turn credit assignment. Two such schemes appear in the recent literature. The first, trajectory-level broadcast , samples G trajectories of M turns per prompt, scores each trajectory by its final reward, computes a trajectory-level advantage, and broadcasts that single advantage uniformly to every turn in the trajectory. The second, pooled per-response ,pools all G × M responses across the G trajectories, treats each response as an independent sample, and computes advantages within the pool. Neither scheme suits our setting. Sequential revision is structurally different from a vanilla multi-turn task: each turn is a fresh attempt at the same problem x, not a step in a longer-horizon decision process with intermediate sub-rewards. Trajectory-level broadcast assigns identical credit to every turn and therefore ignores that some intermediate responses are much closer to correctness than others. Pooled per-response also fails to capture this structure: the wrong intermediate responses are treated identically, even though the near-miss failures are exactly the ones we most want the policy to learn from. Our offline selection-then-replay scheme bypasses both pathologies. We identify near-miss failures explicitly through the success-trajectory filter and replay them as single-turn revision prompts, where standard single-turn credit assignment applies cleanly. 

F.2 Wall-Clock Efficiency 

Beyond avoiding credit-assignment pitfalls, our offline scheme is substantially faster than online multi-turn training. Multi-turn RL pays a length-M serial sequential-revision cost per prompt at every gradient step, multiplied by the G rollouts needed to estimate the advantage. Our method pays the length-K serial cost only once per prompt at the offline stage, and during the online RL stage performs only single-turn sampling on the extracted prompts, which can be parallelized across N Best-of-N samples. Single-turn parallel sampling is much cheaper per gradient step than M-turn serial generation across G rollouts, since serial generation does not benefit from the parallelism that modern inference engines provide for single-turn sampling. Empirically, on 8 × NVIDIA H200 GPUs, three full iterations of our method completed in roughly 30 hours, while the equivalent online multi-turn baseline required over 48 hours on identical hardware (Table 6). With a more carefully optimized infrastructure, Stage I data generation and Stage II RL training could run concurrently (Stage II first trains on the original RL data while Stage I generates augmented data asynchronously), yielding further gains.

Reward shaping. The raw objective of a candidate y is its sum of radii g(y) := ∑ni=1 ri(y)

(a continuous quantity), which clusters in a narrow band near the optimum ( g ≈ 2.63 for the n = 26 instance we evaluate) and differs across candidates only at the third or fourth decimal. Used directly, this signal is too compressed to drive learning. We therefore shape it in two steps. Let v(y) ≥ 0 denote the total constraint violation (circle overlap plus boundary excess), so that y is feasible iff v(y) = 0. First, define the raw score s(y) as

−1, v(y) > 0 and g(y) > 2.64, i.e., minor violations are absorbed as a soft penalty, while implausibly high sums coupled with infeasibility are flagged as cheating and assigned s = −1. Second, we amplify by squaring the offset from 2.5: R(s) := ( s − 2.5 )2. Since feasible raw scores live above 2.5, this magnifies small absolute differences, e.g., a ∆s = 10 −3 gain near s = 2.6 produces a

U(y | x) := R(s(y)) − R(s(x)) ,so the policy is rewarded for any move that improves on the parent and penalized for any move that worsens it. 

Stage I: search rollouts and prompt-set construction. We run 4 sequential-revision rollouts under the current πθ with budget K = 100, where each step proposes a refinement of the previous candidate configuration and the verifier returns the shaped reward U(y | x)

defined above. From the visited intermediate states across these rollouts, we filter to retain those whose subsequent revisions produce verified improvements, and assemble the resulting revision prompts into the augmented prompt set. 

Stage II: RL training. We train Qwen3-4B with GRPO (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2025) on the prompt set from Stage I. Each gradient step samples 8 prompts; for each prompt we draw 64 rollouts to form the group used for the GRPO advantage estimate.
