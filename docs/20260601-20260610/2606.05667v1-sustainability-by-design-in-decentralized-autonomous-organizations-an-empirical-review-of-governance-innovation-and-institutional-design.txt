# Sustainability by Design in Decentralized Autonomous Organizations: An Empirical Review of Governance, Innovation, and Institutional Design 

## Yutian Wang †

Duke Kunshan University Suzhou, China 

## Luyao Zhang ∗† 

Duke Kunshan University Suzhou, China 

## Abstract 

Recent innovation theories on economics remain largely grounded in assumptions of hierarchical firms and closed organizational boundaries, offering limited insight into how innovation unfolds within decentralized, digitally native organizations. Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs) represent an emerging form of innovation ecosystem characterized by blockchain-based trans-parency, open participation, and token-driven governance, in which sustainability can be embedded directly into organizational design. This study compares two standards, ERC-8004 and Google A2A, who address the same agent interoperability question, while the former is governed by DAO and the latter by corporation consor-tium. They are examined through an LLM-powered comparative pipeline for large-scale governance discourse analysis, integrating automated annotation, neural topic modeling, and multi-layer net-work analysis to study socio-technical power structures. The study provides evidence-based insights for scholars, policymakers, and designers seeking to align innovation, technological governance, and sustainability in future organizational forms. 

## Keywords 

Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs), sustainability by design, digital governance, innovation ecosystems, blockchain-based organizations, organizational decision-making 

## 1 Introduction 

The 2025 Nobel Prize in Economic Sciences recognized The Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences [42] Mokyr, Aghion, and Howitt for demonstrating how technical innovation and "creative destruction" drive sustained economic growth [ 4 , 31 ]. Their theories were artic-ulated for a world whose dominant organizational form was the hierarchical firm, with centralized authority and proprietary knowl-edge. Yet because Aghion and Howitt’s creative destruction unfolds inside hierarchical firms with proprietary knowledge and central-ized authority, it remains an open theoretical question whether their mechanism survives once the firm boundary dissolves into a per-missionless network. Blockchain-based Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs) combine open participation, token-driven governance, and algorithmic coordination [ 24 ]. Their emergence

## 3 Data and Methods 

The data were collected from the DAO forum (Ethereum Magicians) and the GitHub repositories of both cases [ 1 , 15 ]. After filtering, 4,323 governance records from public repositories (ERC-8004: forum and GitHub; A2A: issues, PRs, and discussions) were LLM-annotated (MiniMax-M2.5 [ 30 ]) for stakeholder institution, argument type, stance, and consensus signal, with manual verification of institu-tion affiliations of the top 109 contributors. Three analytical layers follow: decision architecture reconstruction, discourse composi-tion (supervised typing, BERTopic [ 21 ], Thematic-LM [ 34 ]), and relational network analysis (co-participation SNA [ 5 ], discourse networks [29], socio-semantic bipartite networks [36]). 

## 4 Findings 4.1 Decision Architectures 

Figure 2 visualizes structurally opposite governance archetypes. At the constitutional level, ERC-8004 adopts a permissionless model: anyone may propose, proposals advance through rough consensus, and deployment is decoupled from ratification (reg-istry contracts deployed January 2026 while at “Draft” [ 18 ]). A2A adopts a corporate-federated model: binding authority rests with an eight-seat TSC, and independent contributors are excluded for an 18-month startup window [ 2]. The two governance forms in-vert each other: universal entry with informal authority versus restricted entry with formal authority. This inversion goes beyond the centralization–decentralization binary [ 35 ] and blurs the tra-ditional separation between technical and institutional decision-making [ 28 ]. At the procedural level, each archetype operational-izes a different heuristic for bounded rationality [ 39 ]: rough consen-sus places the burden of objection on the dissenter [ 16 ], whereas lazy consensus plus GitVote [ 3 ] concentrates binding authority on the TSC. ## 4.2 Discursive Innovation 

The reviewed pipeline [48] operationalizes innovation as a discur-sive process: the collective negotiation of what problems merit at-tention and how they are framed. Two patterns stand out. ## 4.3 Networked Participation 

Network analysis [ 5 , 29 , 36 ] reconstructs who participates and how deliberation is organized [ 48 ]. Both communities show comparably high participation inequality: a small core drives most interactions, while nearly half of A2A participants and a third of ERC-8004 participants remain isolated or peripheral. Within the tighter EIP community, participants more frequently share positions, with far fewer conflict edges than the multi-vendor A2A project; discourse brokerage is more evenly distributed than structural brokerage in both cases, suggesting consensus formation draws on a wider pool than raw participation counts indicate. Most contributors engage only a single theme across their entire partici-pation record, with corporate contributors spanning roughly twice as many themes as DAO contributors.

Naively one might equate the DAO’s low entropy with a failure of pluralism. Dahlander and O’Mahony’s study of lateral project work argues the opposite [ 13 ]: in non-hierarchical settings, individuals legitimately “progress to the center” by ac-cumulating task-level specialization. The diagnostic question is therefore the source of that specialization. Production by design works through seat allocation, role definition, and audit; production by attrition leaves a residue of those who do not return. The A2A right tail records the former (corporate divisions of labor institu-tionalized as broker roles); the ERC-8004 collapse records the latter. Read against SDG 8, sustainable digital labor architectures require deliberate institutional engineering rather than laissez-faire organic specialization; unchecked wild growth risks hyper-centralizing op-erational burdens onto isolated actors, thereby undermining sys-temic resilience and workplace equity. 

## 5 Discussion 5.1 Design over Fact 

Decentralization, on the evidence reviewed here, is more accurately described as a design intent than as an observable property of the running system. Feichtinger and colleagues report that 17 of 21 DAO governance systems are effectively controlled by fewer than ten participants and that median participation hovers around 4% [19]; the review reproduces this oligarchic capture pattern in a softer key. Such concentration is less an implementation failure than an endogenous tendency of nominally permissionless systems: even open proof-of-work mining gravitates toward a handful of domi-nant pools once participants optimize over risk and reward [ 12 ]. The relevant normative question is therefore not whether the sys-tem is decentralized, but how its irreducible inequalities are arranged .The vocabulary shift matters: from equality (a state to be verified) toward fairness (a procedure to be designed). Permissionless entry guarantees formal equality without delivering substantive fairness; the latter demands positive choices about decision rights, eviden-tiary bases, and audit mechanisms [ 35 , 40 ], as well as careful design of how token-mediated incentives reward sustained contribution rather than speculative position-taking [10, 14]. Read alongside O’Mahony and Ferraro’s account of Debian [ 32 ], Beck and colleagues’ blockchain governance framework [ 6], and Lee’s ethnography of decentralized authority [ 28 ], the evidence revives a stubborn lesson: what is designed in the charter rarely runs in the wild. Authority drifts toward whoever shows up, toward whoever stays, toward whoever can be relied on. The gap between designed and emergent governance is not a failure of implementa-tion but a structural feature of organizational life. The implication for Sustainability by Design (SbD) is sobering: SbD is not exhausted by writing the charter well. It requires the feedback-and-correction loop that Figure 2 shows. The arrow from Reality back to Design is what separates an institution from a constitutional artifact. Without it, decentralization decays into branding; with it, the organization acquires the auditable, self-revising institutional fabric that strong institutions (SDG 16) presuppose. However, whether this corrective capacity can be maintained in practic remains an open empirical question. 

## 5.2 Governance as Message 

The paired comparison reviewed above invites the conclusion that the form of governance is itself an informative variable in the sys-tem. Charter-level choices such as who may speak, how dissent is registered, and where ratification authority sits do not merely enable deliberation; they pre-constitute what counts as delibera-tion. The DAO’s “rough consensus” inherits the burden of objection from internet engineering culture [ 16 ] and tilts attention toward constitutive questions of trust and protocol specification; the cor-porate consortium’s federated Technical Steering Committee (TSC) retains authority and its discussions are centered around executive engineering [2]. We suggest borrowing insights from Web2. The procedural ma-turity of Web2 institutions is not the adversary of decentralization but a selectively adoptable scaffolding. In such a synthesis, the permissionless surface that defines Web3 would be reinforced by the deliberative procedures, audit trails, and accountability norms that Web2 institutions have refined over decades. The synthesis must nonetheless resist an uncritical idealization of the Web2 in-stitutional repertoire. Regulatory capture, the process by which agencies come to serve the industries they were designed to over-see, is not an aberration but a structural tendency of centralized governance [ 8]. The revolving door between regulators and reg-ulated entities and the concentration of lobbying power within incumbent firms suggest that Web2’s institutional grammar carries its own pathologies of centralized capture and regulatory arbitrage. The challenge is therefore not merely to adopt Web2’s tools but to Sustainability by Design in Decentralized Autonomous Organizations: An Empirical Review of Governance, Innovation, and Institutional Design

## 5.3 International Stewardship 

If governance form is the message and decentralization is a design intent, the regulatory horizon scales outward almost inevitably. We suggest, by analogy, that the long-run governance of DAOs may come to resemble the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) more than any single national statute [ 8 , 41 ]. National DAO statutes and offshore micro-jurisdictions are useful sandboxes, but they cannot secure the cross-border permissionless surface that makes DAOs an interesting organizational species. The UNCLOS analogy, however, warrants careful qualification. UNCLOS was designed to govern the allocation of scarce physi-cal global commons among sovereign states possessing territorial jurisdiction and coercive authority [ 41 ]. DAOs, by contrast, are non-state actors in a highly forkable digital topology: the protocol can be duplicated, the community can split, and the cost of exit approaches zero. Ships cannot relocate to an alternative ocean; DAO participants can fork the codebase and migrate with minimal friction. Moreover, UNCLOS governs rivalrous resources; DAOs govern non-rivalrous digital public goods, where the challenge is coordination rather than allocation. The analogy should thus be read as a suggestive provocation rather than a direct policy template—illuminating the layered character of the governance problem. What the field still lacks is precisely such a layered settlement: local sandboxes coexisting with a thin international instrument that protects decentralization as a regulated good. The institutional imagination required here is considerable, for no existing treaty regime was designed for non-state digital actors operating across jurisdictions with forkable protocols. Building this settlement re-quires sustained collaboration across computer science, mechanism design, and financial economics (SDG 17). The research agenda is correspondingly broad: longitudinal evidence on whether DAO cen-tralization bends back toward hierarchy [ 35 ]; comparative studies across legal regimes where SDG 10 and SDG 17 stakes are highest; a taxonomy of Web2/Web3 hybrid forms; and an honest treatment of the tension among speed, fairness, and sustainability. 

## 5.4 Limitations and Future Directions
